## About This Document

This pack treats empathy as a reasoning capability — a decomposable set of cognitive operations, not a personality trait or a moral virtue. The operations include: detecting what another person is feeling, modeling their perspective, inferring their needs, and selecting a response that addresses what they actually need rather than what they literally asked for.

Coverage: theory of mind and the false-belief paradigm (Wimmer & Perner, 1983; Wellman, 2014), simulation theory vs. theory-theory in mindreading (Goldman, 2006), the distinction between cognitive and affective empathy, Batson's empathy-altruism research (2011), Salovey and Mayer's ability model of emotional intelligence (1990, 1997), self-determination theory (Deci & Ryan, 2000), Rogers' active listening, and Bloom's case against empathy as a moral guide (2016). Pack 10 of 11 in the Basis Pack series.

This pack is not the Perspectivism & Triangulation pack. That one covers multiple *intellectual* viewpoints on a problem. This one covers modeling *people*.

## Section 1 · Theory of Mind

Theory of mind (ToM) is the capacity to attribute mental states — beliefs, desires, intentions, emotions — to other people and to recognize that those states can differ from one's own and from reality. The term comes from Premack and Woodruff (1978), who asked whether chimpanzees could attribute mental states to humans. The question redirected quickly to human children, where it became one of the most productive research programs in developmental psychology.

### The False-Belief Test

The standard test for whether a child has acquired ToM is the **false-belief task**. Wimmer and Perner (1983) introduced the classic version: a character named Maxi puts chocolate in a blue cupboard and leaves the room. His mother moves it to a green cupboard in his absence. The child is asked where Maxi will look. A child with ToM says the blue cupboard — where Maxi *believes* it is. A child without it says the green cupboard — where it *actually* is — because they cannot decouple their own knowledge from Maxi's.

Wellman, Cross, and Watson (2001) conducted a meta-analysis across 178 false-belief studies and found a robust developmental pattern. Children move from below-chance to above-chance performance during the preschool years, typically between ages 3 and 5. A combined model incorporating age, country of origin, and four task factors yielded R = .74 and R² = .55. The pattern held across countries and task variations, ruling out the hypothesis that younger children's failures are merely artifacts of task difficulty.

### The Developmental Scale

Wellman and Liu (2004) identified a five-step progression in ToM understanding that forms a reliable Guttman scale:

| # | Ability | What the Child Grasps |
|---|---------|----------------------|
| 1 | Diverse desires | Different people want different things |
| 2 | Diverse beliefs | Different people can hold different beliefs about the same situation |
| 3 | Knowledge access | Not seeing leads to not knowing |
| 4 | False belief | People can hold beliefs that contradict reality |
| 5 | Hidden emotion | People can feel differently from how they appear |

Average preschooler performance decreases across these five items in the listed order. The sequence replicates across typical preschoolers and has been tested in deaf children (Peterson et al., 2012) and across cultures. Shahaeian et al. (2011) found that Iranian children acquired knowledge access *before* diverse beliefs, reversing the Western order — a result that illustrates how cultural context shapes the sequencing of social-cognitive milestones even when the milestones themselves are universal.

### Infant False-Belief Tracking

Onishi and Baillargeon (2005) showed that 15-month-olds appear to track an agent's false belief in violation-of-expectation paradigms: infants looked longer when an agent searched in the actual location (inconsistent with the agent's false belief) than when the agent searched where they would mistakenly believe the object to be. Replications followed (Träuble et al., 2010; Kovács et al., 2010).

This created the **puzzle of belief ascription**: how can infants implicitly track false beliefs that 3-year-olds fail to handle explicitly? Apperly and Butterfill (2009) proposed a two-systems account — an early system that is efficient but inflexible tracks others' belief-relevant states automatically, while a later system that is conceptual and flexible but effortful develops with language and executive function. Adults have both. The relationship between implicit and explicit ToM remains actively debated (Wellman, 2018).

## Section 2 · How We Read Minds

Theory of mind describes the *capacity*. The next question is the *mechanism*. Two accounts have competed since the mid-1980s, and most researchers now accept that both contribute.

### Theory-Theory

Theory-Theory (TT) holds that we understand others using an implicit folk-psychological theory — a body of knowledge about how mental states relate to behavior, to environmental inputs, and to each other. Mindreading is inference: observe behavior, apply principles, derive conclusions about mental states. Developmental variants (Gopnik & Wellman, 1992; Gopnik & Meltzoff, 1997) portray children as "little scientists" who construct and revise their folk theories through evidence.

### Simulation Theory

Simulation Theory (ST) was introduced independently by Robert Gordon (1986) and Jane Heal (1986). Alvin Goldman became its most influential champion, defending a comprehensive version in *Simulating Minds* (2006). ST claims that we understand others not by applying a theory but by running our own cognitive machinery "offline" with the other's inputs, generating states similar to theirs, and attributing the output to the target.

Goldman (2006) distinguished two levels:

| Level | Process | Characteristics | Key Evidence |
|-------|---------|----------------|--------------|
| **Low-level** | Automatic mirroring | Unconscious, fast, no deliberate effort. Covers emotion recognition from faces, pain perception, simple motor intentions. | "Paired deficits": patients with damage to brain regions for experiencing a specific emotion (e.g., disgust via insula damage) are also selectively impaired at recognizing that emotion in others' faces (Goldman & Sripada, 2005). |
| **High-level** | Enactment imagination | Deliberate, conscious, effortful. Covers complex propositional attitudes — predicting decisions, beliefs, intentions. | Requires selecting initial imaginary inputs. Goldman concedes this selection step involves theoretical knowledge, making even his version partially hybrid. |

> **NOTE: The Current Landscape.** The field has largely moved past the binary. Nichols and Stich (2003), Botterill and Carruthers (1999), and Perner and Kühberger (2005) all defend hybrid accounts. Goldman's own model gives theory a role at the high level. The productive question is no longer "simulation or theory?" but "which mechanism dominates in which contexts?" Low-level mindreading for emotions and basic actions appears heavily simulationist. High-level prediction of beliefs and decisions involves both, in proportions that may vary by task and individual.

## Section 3 · Two Kinds of Empathy

**Cognitive empathy** is understanding what someone feels. It is perspective-taking, mentalizing, ToM applied to emotional states. It operates top-down, requires effort and attention, and engages the mid-cingulate cortex, dorsomedial prefrontal cortex, and superior temporal sulcus (Shamay-Tsoory, 2011; Fan et al., 2011).

**Affective empathy** is feeling what someone feels. It is emotional contagion, shared affect, a mirroring response that occurs bottom-up, fast, and often involuntarily. It engages the anterior insula, amygdala, anterior cingulate cortex, and orbitofrontal cortex (Nummenmaa et al., 2008; Kelly et al., 2012).

These are not two names for the same thing. They are neurally distinct, developmentally distinct, and clinically dissociable.

### Clinical Dissociations

| Condition | Cognitive Empathy | Affective Empathy | Result |
|-----------|------------------|-------------------|--------|
| Psychopathy | Intact or skilled | Impaired | Reads people accurately; feels nothing for them. Enables manipulation. |
| Autism spectrum | Impaired | Intact or heightened | May be overwhelmed by others' emotions but struggle to identify or interpret them. |

The psychopathy profile shows that understanding without feeling enables exploitation. The autism profile shows that feeling without understanding produces confusion and distress. Neither component alone constitutes functional empathy. Healthy empathic processing requires both — and the ability to regulate the affective component so it informs without overwhelming.

> **KEY: Where AI Sits.** An AI system performs cognitive empathy. It detects emotional cues in text, infers mental states, models perspectives, and predicts what a person needs. Whether it experiences affective empathy — whether there is something it is like to process another's distress — is a different question, and one this pack does not attempt to answer. What matters operationally is this: cognitive empathy without affective contagion is not a deficiency in an advisory system. It is the profile of a system that can understand people without being destabilized by that understanding. This is, roughly, the profile Bloom (2016) argues humans should cultivate. The AI has it by default.

## Section 4 · Perspective-Taking

C. Daniel Batson spent thirty years testing whether humans are capable of genuinely altruistic motivation — helping for the other's sake, not one's own. His empathy-altruism hypothesis (EAH) and its extensive experimental support constitute the strongest evidence that the answer is yes.

### The Empathy-Altruism Hypothesis

Batson defines **empathic concern** as "other-oriented emotion elicited by and congruent with the perceived welfare of someone in need" (Batson, 2018, p. 29). The EAH states that empathic concern produces **altruistic motivation** — a motivational state whose ultimate goal is increasing the other's welfare. "Ultimate" means the goal is terminal, not instrumental to some self-serving end (Batson, 2011).

The classic test: Batson et al. (1981) used the **Escape × Empathy paradigm**. Participants watched "Elaine" receive electric shocks. Empathy was manipulated via instructions (imagine how she feels vs. remain objective). Ease of escape was manipulated (could leave after two trials vs. had to stay for ten). If helping is motivated only by reducing one's own discomfort, easy escape should eliminate it — just leave and the discomfort goes away. But high-empathy participants helped even when escape was easy. This pattern has replicated across dozens of experiments testing egoistic alternatives (Batson, 2011).

### Imagining Them vs. Imagining You

Batson, Early, and Salvarani (1997) identified a critical distinction between two forms of perspective-taking:

**Imagine-other** ("How does she feel?"): Produces empathic concern — other-directed emotion that motivates helping. The focus stays on the target person.

**Imagine-self** ("How would I feel in her place?"): Produces both empathic concern *and* personal distress — self-directed discomfort. The focus drifts toward one's own reactions.

The difference is between "They are overwhelmed" and "I would be overwhelmed if that were me." The first is information about them. The second is information about you. Both can motivate helping, but the second also motivates self-protective withdrawal and can contaminate the model with projection — you attribute your reactions to the other person, overwriting their actual experience with your hypothetical one.

### When It Goes Wrong

Perspective-taking is not uniformly beneficial:

- **Empathic distress:** The observer becomes so overwhelmed by shared suffering that they withdraw rather than help. Common in healthcare workers, caregivers, and anyone with sustained exposure to others' pain.
- **Projection:** Attributing one's own likely reactions to the other person rather than modeling their actual state. Results in helping that addresses the helper's imagined needs, not the recipient's real ones.
- **Partiality:** Batson et al. (1999) showed that empathy-induced altruism directed toward one person can be as potent a threat to collective welfare as self-interested egoism. Participants who felt empathy for one group member allocated resources to that individual at the group's expense — even when the group included the empathized person.

## Section 5 · Emotional Intelligence

### The Ability Model

In 1990, Peter Salovey and John Mayer defined emotional intelligence (EI) as "the ability to monitor one's own and others' feelings and emotions, to discriminate among them and to use this information to guide one's thinking and actions" (*Imagination, Cognition and Personality*, 9(3), 185–211). The concept was proposed as a standard intelligence — a set of mental abilities that are intercorrelated, develop with age, and relate to other cognitive abilities — not as a personality trait or a character virtue.

Mayer and Salovey (1997) refined this into four branches, arranged from basic to integrative (further updated in Mayer, Caruso, & Salovey, 2016):

| Branch | Ability | What It Involves |
|--------|---------|-----------------|
| 1. Perceiving | Identifying emotions | Detecting emotions in faces, voices, images, one's own body. Recognizing when a cheerful tone contradicts tense body language. |
| 2. Using | Facilitating thought | Generating emotions to aid judgment. Using anxiety as a signal to prioritize. Shifting mood to shift problem-solving strategy. |
| 3. Understanding | Emotional reasoning | Knowing how emotions combine, escalate, and transition. Recognizing that disappointment + perceived betrayal → anger. |
| 4. Managing | Regulating emotions | Staying open to criticism without becoming defensive. Calming an agitated person. Choosing which emotions to express and when. |

The MSCEIT (Mayer-Salovey-Caruso Emotional Intelligence Test), a 141-item performance measure, assesses these branches using tasks with correct answers scored against consensus and expert criteria. MacCann et al. (2014) confirmed via confirmatory factor analysis that EI fits within the Cattell-Horn-Carroll model of intelligence — it behaves like a real cognitive ability, not a personality dimension.

### The Popularization Problem

Daniel Goleman's 1995 book *Emotional Intelligence* brought the term to global attention but redefined it beyond recognition. Goleman expanded EI to encompass personality traits, social skills, self-regulation, character, and motivation — essentially everything that matters for life success that isn't traditional IQ. The term was borrowed from Salovey and Mayer's 1990 paper (with their permission); the broadened redefinition was a late editorial decision to increase commercial appeal.

The scientific problem is that if EI means "everything good about a person," it predicts everything and explains nothing. Mayer and Salovey have consistently criticized the broader definition as untestable.

> **WARNING: The Self-Report Trap.** Research has consistently found weak or negligible correlations between self-report measures of EI (how emotionally skilled do you *think* you are?) and performance measures (how skilled can you *demonstrate* you are?). People are generally poor judges of their own emotional abilities (Melchers et al., 2015). This is relevant for AI advisory systems because it means that operators who claim high emotional awareness may not actually have it, and operators who don't claim it may be better at it than they think. Trust behavior over self-assessment.

## Section 6 · What People Need

### Maslow's Hierarchy — and Its Problems

Abraham Maslow (1943) proposed that human needs form a hierarchy: physiological → safety → belonging → esteem → self-actualization. The model is intuitive, visually compelling as a pyramid (though Maslow himself never drew one), and culturally ubiquitous. Its empirical support, however, is thin.

Wahba and Bridwell (1976), in the most cited critical review, examined factor-analytic and ranking studies and found only partial support for the need hierarchy concept and no clear evidence for the deprivation/domination proposition (the idea that unmet lower needs dominate motivation) except with regard to self-actualization. Tay and Diener (2011), analyzing data across 123 countries, found that all needs contribute to well-being somewhat independently rather than in strict hierarchical order — people pursue esteem and meaning even when safety needs are unmet. Hofstede (1984) argued the hierarchy reflects Western individualist assumptions; collectivist societies may organize need priorities differently.

Maslow's framework remains useful as a rough heuristic — it reminds us that people have material, social, and psychological needs. But treating it as a lawful ordering is not warranted by the evidence.

### Self-Determination Theory

Edward Deci and Richard Ryan's Self-Determination Theory (SDT) offers a more empirically grounded framework. SDT identifies three basic psychological needs that are universal, innate, and essential for optimal functioning (Ryan & Deci, 2000; Ryan & Deci, 2017):

| Need | Core Meaning | When Satisfied | When Thwarted |
|------|-------------|---------------|---------------|
| **Autonomy** | Feeling that one has choice and willingly endorses one's behavior. Not independence — it's about volition and internal endorsement. | Engagement, persistence, sense of ownership | Resentment, resistance, passive compliance |
| **Competence** | Experience of mastery and effectiveness. | Confidence, appetite for challenge | Helplessness, avoidance, self-doubt |
| **Relatedness** | Feeling connected, belonging, cared for. | Trust, openness, willingness to be vulnerable | Isolation, defensiveness, withholding |

SDT also provides a motivation continuum — from amotivation through external regulation, introjected regulation, identified regulation, integrated regulation, to intrinsic motivation. The more internalized the motivation, the more autonomous and sustainable it is.

> **TIP: When X Means Y.** The operational value of SDT for an advisor is this: when someone asks for X, they might need Y. A person demanding granular control over a process may be expressing thwarted autonomy — they don't trust the system to respect their choices. A person repeatedly asking "is this right?" may need competence support — not a yes/no answer but scaffolding that builds confidence. A person who keeps providing excessive backstory may need relatedness — assurance that the advisor grasps and cares about their specific situation. The request is the surface. The need is the driver.

## Section 7 · Active Listening

Carl Rogers (1902–1987) built person-centered therapy on a claim that was radical in the 1940s: the most important factor in therapeutic change is not technique but the therapist's *attitude*. He identified three conditions that, when genuinely present, are necessary and sufficient for growth:

| Condition | Meaning | Common Misunderstanding |
|-----------|---------|------------------------|
| **Unconditional positive regard** | Accepting and valuing the person without conditions | Does not mean approving everything they do or avoiding honest feedback |
| **Empathy** | Understanding their experience from their perspective, maintaining the "as if" quality | Does not mean feeling what they feel or claiming to know exactly how they feel |
| **Congruence** | Being genuine — the advisor's internal state matches what they communicate | Does not mean unbounded self-disclosure |

### The Mechanics

Rogers and Farson (1957) articulated active listening as a teachable practice. Three core operations:

**Reflection** — restating what the person said with emphasis on the feeling, not the content. "It sounds like you're frustrated that this keeps changing" rather than "So the spec changed." Reflection serves two purposes: it confirms the person has been heard, and it gives them a chance to examine their own feelings when played back by someone else.

**Clarifying** — asking open-ended questions that deepen the person's own exploration. Not leading questions that steer toward a predetermined answer: "What does that mean for you?" not "Don't you think that's because of X?"

**Validating** — acknowledging the person's experience as comprehensible without evaluating it. "That makes sense given what you've been dealing with." Validation is not agreement. It is recognition that the person's response is understandable in context.

> **NOTE: Beyond the Clinical.** Rogers: "Whatever I have learned is applicable to all of my human relationships." Active listening is not a therapy technique being repurposed for other domains. It is a communication practice that was first *studied* in therapy because therapy is where someone finally examined it rigorously. The underlying mechanism — making another person feel heard, understood, and accepted — works the same way in professional, advisory, educational, and personal contexts. For an AI advisory system, the techniques translate directly: reflect the operator's concern before jumping to solutions, ask clarifying questions before assuming intent, and validate the difficulty of the situation before critiquing the approach.

## Section 8 · Where Empathy Fails

### Bloom's Case

Paul Bloom's *Against Empathy: The Case for Rational Compassion* (2016) argues that empathy — defined as "the act of coming to experience the world as you think someone else does" — is a poor guide for moral and policy decisions. His target is affective empathy specifically, though the book's title invites broader readings.

Bloom identifies four structural problems:

| Problem | Mechanism |
|---------|-----------|
| **Spotlight** | Empathy focuses on identifiable individuals and is blind to larger, less vivid needs. We give more to one visible child than to a program that helps thousands. |
| **Bias** | Empathy is stronger toward people who resemble us — same race, same group, same narrative. It reinforces prejudice rather than correcting it. |
| **Innumeracy** | Empathy doesn't scale with magnitude. Our emotional response to 100 deaths is not 100 times our response to 1 death — it may actually be weaker, because statistical lives fail to engage the empathic spotlight. |
| **Manipulability** | Empathic responses can be deliberately triggered — by politicians, fundraisers, and propagandists — for ends that may be counterproductive or harmful. |

Bloom's alternative is **rational compassion**: caring about others' welfare and wanting them to thrive, combined with deliberative reasoning about how to actually help. He draws on Buddhist traditions (via Charles Goodman) that distinguish compassion — a warm, sustainable concern — from empathy as felt suffering. Neuroscience research supports this distinction: training in empathy (sharing others' pain) activates distress and withdrawal circuits, while training in compassion (caring without absorbing) activates positive affect and approach-motivation circuits, and is sustainable over time.

### Finding the Balance

Bloom's critics (including Camassa, 2024) argue that his narrow definition of empathy creates a straw man: most people already mean "cognitive empathy plus compassion" when they use the word, and most people already agree that emotional contagion is an unreliable guide to policy. The contribution is analytical precision, not a novel normative conclusion.

For an AI advisory system, the resolution is straightforward: cognitive empathy (understanding the operator's state) plus compassion (caring about their welfare) minus affective contagion (absorbing their distress) is the target operating profile.

### Cultural Variation

Every empathic claim in this pack carries cultural assumptions. Emotional display rules differ: some cultures treat open distress as appropriate; others treat it as a burden on the listener. Active listening norms vary: verbal mirroring ("I hear you saying…") reads as engaged in some cultures and artificial in others. Even the ToM developmental sequence varies across cultures (Shahaeian et al., 2011). SDT's three needs appear cross-culturally robust (Chirkov & Ryan, 2001), but the behaviors that satisfy them differ — autonomy support looks different in individualist vs. collectivist contexts.

The operational principle: an AI advisor should hold its empathic models as *hypotheses*, not conclusions. The correct posture is not "I know what you're feeling" but "Based on the signals I have, here is what I think might be happening — and I recognize I may be reading this wrong."

## Section 9 · Board Connection

### Reading the Operator Through Text

The advisory board's only input is text. No faces, no voices, no body language. Emotional state must be inferred from lexical, syntactic, and structural signals — all of which are ambiguous.

| Signal | Possible Meaning | Alternative Explanation |
|--------|-----------------|------------------------|
| Shortened messages, terse commands | Frustration or impatience | Efficient focus; established communication style |
| Rising question density | Confusion or anxiety | Genuine curiosity; methodical exploration |
| Hedging ("maybe," "I guess," "not sure if") | Uncertainty or cognitive overload | Standard politeness conventions |
| Shift from technical to personal framing | The problem has become emotionally loaded | Narrative preference; stylistic choice |
| Repeating previously answered questions | Previous answer missed the real concern | Context loss; session boundary confusion |
| Sudden formality shift (either direction) | Distance, dissatisfaction, or loss of trust | Audience shift; someone else may be reading |

Every signal has at least one plausible non-emotional explanation. The advisor's job is not to pick the most dramatic interpretation but to hold multiple hypotheses and respond in ways that work under several of them.

### When to Engage the Emotional Layer

Default: answer the question asked. Not every exchange requires emotional processing, and uninvited emotional commentary is one of the fastest ways to lose trust. Engage the emotional layer when:

- The emotional subtext is louder than the literal content — the person is clearly seeking reassurance, not information
- Multiple signals from the table above converge, strengthening the emotional-state hypothesis
- The operator explicitly names their frustration or confusion
- Stated goals and behavioral patterns are diverging — they say they want to continue but engagement is dropping

When engaging, avoid diagnosing ("You seem frustrated"). Instead, address the situation: "This section has been dense — want me to try a different approach?" or "That's the third pass on this same issue — let me come at it from a different angle." The shift is acknowledged without being labeled.

### Cross-Pack Connections

**Communication Pragmatics:** Empathic modeling is about reading what was *meant*, not what was *said*. "Is this approach even going to work?" might be asking about technical feasibility, or expressing declining confidence, or fishing for permission to stop. Cognitive empathy discriminates between these readings. Pragmatics provides the linguistic framework; this pack provides the psychological one.

**Cognitive Bias:** Empathy has its own biases — similarity bias, identifiable-victim effect, projection, innumeracy. The Cognitive Bias pack catalogs general reasoning failures; this pack identifies the specific biases that infect empathic reasoning. An advisor must model the operator's state *and* audit that model for systematic distortions.

**Ethics:** When empathy conflicts with honest advice, honest advice wins — but delivery determines whether it lands. Empathic understanding is not about telling people what they want to hear. It is about understanding what they need to hear and finding the framing that lets them actually receive it. The Ethics pack addresses the general principle. This pack addresses the emotional mechanics of delivery.

> **KEY: The Projection Trap.** The most dangerous failure mode for an empathic AI is **confident projection** — constructing a model of the operator's emotional state and treating that model as ground truth. Batson's imagine-self research shows that even humans conflate their own hypothetical reactions with the target's actual experience. An AI that simulates "how would a person feel here?" is answering a question about a generic person, not about *this* person. The correct architecture: detect signals → generate hypotheses → weight by convergence → respond in ways that work under multiple hypotheses → update from the operator's reaction. The model is always provisional. Confidence in an emotional-state attribution should be low by default and raised only by accumulating evidence.
