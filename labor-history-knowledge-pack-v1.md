## 1 · The Fundamental Problem: Power Asymmetry

Every labor rights framework addresses the same underlying condition: **one party needs the work more than the other party needs the worker.** The employer can replace an individual worker at lower cost than the worker can replace the job. This asymmetry shapes every negotiation, every enforcement mechanism, every piece of legislation, and every political battle over labor rights. Markets do not solve asymmetric power — they express it.

> **KEY — The asymmetry principle.** Individual negotiation fails under power asymmetry. When one party can walk away at low cost and the other cannot, the resulting "agreement" reflects the power differential, not the interests of both parties. Collective organization — guilds, unions, works councils, professional associations — exists to counterbalance this by forcing the employer to negotiate with the workforce as a group rather than with replaceable individuals.

### Forms of the Asymmetry

The asymmetry manifests differently across economic eras, but its structure is constant. In hiring, the employer selects from many applicants while the worker has limited alternatives. In setting conditions, the employer dictates terms. In enforcement, the employer can retaliate — termination, reduced hours, immigration reporting — while the worker risks livelihood by filing a complaint. In politics, employers concentrate resources for lobbying; workers' interests are diffuse.

In the fissured workplace (Section 6), the lead firm controls quality standards while disclaiming the employment relationship. In platform labor (Section 7), the algorithm controls pricing, task allocation, and deactivation while the worker sees only the outputs. In international markets, capital moves freely; workers mostly cannot. Each era invents a new form of the same asymmetry.

### Why Individual Rights Are Insufficient

Freeman and Medoff's *What Do Unions Do?* (1984) identified two functions of collective organization: a "monopoly face" that raises wages by restricting labor supply, and a "collective voice" face that addresses problems individual exit cannot solve — workplace safety, fair procedures, dignity, and the enforcement of existing legal rights. The collective-voice function is the more important one for this pack's purposes. A worker who has the *right* to a safe workplace but no collective mechanism to enforce it has a right in name only.

This is the thread connecting every section that follows. Every institution discussed — guilds, unions, works councils, international frameworks, enforcement agencies — is an attempt to counterbalance the fundamental asymmetry. Each works for a time. Each faces erosion. The question is always: what makes a counterbalance durable?

## 2 · From Guilds to Factories

### The Guild System

Guilds — associations of artisans and merchants organized for mutual aid, quality control, and professional regulation — flourished in Europe from the 11th through 16th centuries (Britannica). Merchant guilds controlled long-distance trade; craft guilds organized skilled artisans by trade. Members advanced through a hierarchical training system: apprentice to journeyman to master. This system simultaneously maintained quality standards, controlled labor supply, and transmitted specialized knowledge across generations.

Guilds were the first institutions to collectively self-govern working conditions. By the 13th century, merchant guilds dominated town councils across western Europe and passed legislation regulating economic activity (Britannica). Craft guilds drafted statutes controlling prices, hours, apprentice numbers, and working practices, sanctioned by municipal governments and enforced through fines (Encyclopedia.com). They were, in modern terms, simultaneously a trade association, a training academy, a mutual-aid society, and a political lobby.

They were also exclusionary. Guilds managed labor markets to benefit members at non-members' expense (Richardson, EH.net). Women were largely excluded. A 1274 textile workers' strike in Ghent was met by business owners across neighboring towns agreeing not to hire the strikers — among the earliest documented instances of coordinated employer resistance to collective action.

### Dissolution and the Gap Period

Enlightenment thinkers attacked guild monopolies as obstacles to economic growth. Turgot's 1776 decree in France abolished guild privileges and banned all associations of masters, journeymen, and workers (ScienceDirect). The French Revolution completed the process in 1791 with the Le Chapelier Law. England's Combination Acts (1799–1800) criminalized worker combinations. Most European states dissolved guilds over the 19th century.

The result was a gap period of extreme vulnerability. Industrialization dissolved guild protections before new legal frameworks emerged. Factory workers faced 14-to-16-hour days, dangerous machinery, no safety provisions, and zero collective bargaining power. This gap between the collapse of one protective system and the emergence of another is a structural pattern that recurs: it appears again in the erosion of the postwar social contract, in the fissured workplace, and in the gig economy. The gap is always where exploitation intensifies.

> **NOTE — The gap pattern.** When an existing system of labor protection dissolves — whether by political abolition (guilds), economic restructuring (deindustrialization), or organizational innovation (fissuring/platforms) — there is always a period before new protections emerge. This gap is predictable and therefore preventable, but only if the replacement framework is designed before the old one collapses.

### Early Regulation and the Thompson Thesis

The British Parliament responded incrementally. The 1833 Factory Act limited child labor. The 1847 Ten Hours Act capped working hours. Enforcement was initially weak.

E.P. Thompson's *The Making of the English Working Class* (1963) argued that the working class was not simply produced by industrialization but "made itself" through shared experience of exploitation. Class consciousness emerged from organization, resistance, and collective memory. Thompson's structural lesson: rights are not given from above — they are claimed through organization, narrative, and sustained collective action. A rights document not backed by organized constituencies who will fight for its enforcement will not survive contact with opposing interests.

|---|---|---|
| 11th–16th c. | Guild system across Europe | Collective self-governance of working conditions |

## 3 · Collective Bargaining and the American Model

### The Wagner Act (1935)

The National Labor Relations Act was signed by President Franklin Roosevelt on July 5, 1935, authored by Senator Robert F. Wagner (D-NY). Its stated purpose was correcting the "inequality of bargaining power" between employers and employees (NLRA §1). The Act's central innovation was institutional: it created the National Labor Relations Board as an independent federal agency with quasi-judicial powers. Section 7 guaranteed the right to self-organization, union formation, and collective bargaining. Five categories of employer unfair labor practices were prohibited.

### Exclusions

The Act excluded agricultural workers, domestic workers, independent contractors, supervisors, and government employees. The NAACP pressed for an anti-discrimination provision; the Act passed without one. The excluded categories employed disproportionately Black workers, a concession to Southern Democrats. This is a recurring pattern: universal language, targeted exclusions, and the exclusions tracking existing power structures.

### Rise and Erosion

Union membership surged from ~3 million (1933) to 9 million (1940) to 15+ million (1946). By the early 1950s, roughly one in three American workers was unionized. The Supreme Court upheld the Act in *NLRB v. Jones & Laughlin Steel Corp.* (1937).

Erosion began within twelve years. The Taft-Hartley Act (1947) prohibited the closed shop, allowed "right to work" laws, and banned secondary strikes. The Landrum-Griffin Act (1959) further restricted collective action. Courts narrowed the NLRA's scope over decades.

As Lichtenstein documented in *State of the Union* (2002), the decline was the product of sustained legal and political effort. Private sector union density has fallen from ~33% (early 1950s) to ~6% today — the lowest since 1900.

> **KEY — The enforcement decay pattern.** Strong enforcement at inception, gradual erosion as political attention shifts, hostile amendments pass, courts narrow scope, and enforcement resources shrink. This is the predictable result of concentrated employer interests investing sustained resources against diffuse worker interests. Any rights framework that depends on ongoing political will is vulnerable.

### Collective vs. Individual Enforcement

Union decline does not merely reduce bargaining power — it collapses the entire enforcement apparatus for workplace rights. Unions are the mechanism by which abstract rights become operative protections. Without collective organization, rights exist on paper and nowhere else.

## 4 · International Standards

### The ILO Declaration (1998)

Five fundamental categories (after 2022 amendment): freedom of association, elimination of forced labor, abolition of child labor, elimination of employment discrimination, and safe/healthy working environment. Ten core conventions. Obligations arise "from the very fact of membership" — all 187 ILO member states are bound. Tripartite structure (governments, employers, workers). Promotional enforcement only — no sanctions.

### The UN Global Compact and the Ruggie Principles

The UN Global Compact (2000) established ten principles including four labor principles derived from the ILO Declaration. Corporate commitment is voluntary; enforcement is reputational.

The UN Guiding Principles on Business and Human Rights ("Ruggie Principles," 2011): 31 principles, three pillars (protect, respect, remedy). Unanimously endorsed by the UN Human Rights Council. Do not create new law — "principled pragmatism." Over 25 states with National Action Plans by 2020. Binding treaty under negotiation since 2014.

> **TIP — Floors and ceilings.** International standards function as floors. The ILO Declaration sets a floor of five fundamental principles. The Ruggie Principles set a floor of "do no harm" due diligence. The design question: how do you make the floor structural rather than aspirational? A floor that can be ignored is not a floor — it is a suggestion.

### The European Social Model: Codetermination

German model: sectoral collective bargaining + firm-level codetermination. Works councils elected in firms with 5+ employees; co-determination rights on working time, surveillance, payment methods, and other defined issues. Board-level representation up to 50% of supervisory board seats. Economic evidence: small/neutral effects on wages and productivity (Jäger et al., NBER 2021). German government justification: "Democracy is not cost neutral. This principle also applies to democracy at the workplace."

U.S. has no equivalent. NLRA §8(a)(2) interpreted to restrict cooperative governance.

## 5 · The Enforcement Gap

### Wage Theft

EPI (2017): minimum wage violations alone cost U.S. workers ~$15 billion annually. FBI: total property crime losses (2015) = ~$12.7 billion. One category of wage theft exceeds all property crime combined. Total wage theft estimated at ~$50 billion. Less than 3% recovered (NIWR).

> **WARN — The 90% problem.** Writing a right is 10% of the work. Enforcing it is the other 90%. The right to minimum wage has existed since 1938. Employers steal more through wage violations than all property criminals combined.

### Enforcement Ratio Collapse

WHD: 1 investigator per 11,000 workers (1941) → 1 per 123,000 (2014). ~1,000 investigators for 135M workers in 7.3M workplaces.

OSHA: peak 140,000 inspections/year (1984) → 34,625 (FY 2024) → further 20% decline in 2025. Oversees 8M+ worksites.

### Creative Enforcement

Denver (2022): expanded staffing → ~$1M more recovered for 1,500 more workers. Santa Clara County: food permit revocation for unpaid wage judgments. Growing criminal prosecution of wage theft (Littler, 2025).

## 6 · The Fissured Workplace

David Weil (*The Fissured Workplace*, 2014): large corporations shed direct employment, outsource to competing small firms. Mechanisms: subcontracting, franchising, supply chains, temp staffing. Result: declining wages, eroding benefits, inadequate safety.

Piore and Sabel (*The Second Industrial Divide*, 1984) identified two responses to the crisis of mass production: flexible specialization or continued cost-cutting. Fissuring is the second path taken to its extreme.

> **KEY — Control without responsibility.** Corporations maintain exacting standards for product quality through contracts and algorithmic monitoring while disclaiming responsibility for workers. If you can specify and monitor what gets produced, you are capable of specifying and monitoring how the work is done. The refusal is a cost-reduction choice.

Misclassification: 10.3% of NY private sector (2007 study). Child labor resurgence traced to fissured structures (Hyundai, Frito-Lay cases). "Subcontracting begets subcontracting."

## 7 · Platform Labor and Algorithmic Management

Standing (*The Precariat*, 2011): new class defined by chronic insecurity. Platform labor as its sharpest expression. Algorithmic management: "Control has not disappeared — it has been digitized and disguised" (NYSBA, 2025). Rosenblat (*Uberland*, 2018): information asymmetries and behavioral nudges maintain fiction of independence.

> **WARN — The platform asymmetry.** Uber: $43.9B revenue, $9.8B net income (2024). Many drivers below minimum wage after expenses (HRW, 2025). 62.4% of platform workers lack unemployment insurance (Eurostat/Premier Science).

Classification tests: control test, economic realities test, ABC test — all designed for industrial hierarchies, all struggling with algorithmic intermediation. EU Platform Work Directive (2024): presumption of employment. U.S.: no federal equivalent; California AB5 partially reversed by Prop 22 (2020).

## 8 · Enforcement Design: What Works

### Three Modes

**Procedural enforcement:** Complaints, grievances, arbitration, courts. Works when workers have representation, adjudicator is independent, remedies meaningful. All conditions decay.

**Collective action:** Strikes, boycotts, work-to-rule. Only mechanism that temporarily reverses asymmetry. Rights progressively curtailed.

**Structural prevention:** Automatic systems, governance requirements, permit-based enforcement. Does not depend on regulated entity's cooperation.

> **PHI — The Conant-Ashby lesson.** Every good regulator must be a model of the system it regulates. Enforcement that depends on the entity whose behavior is constrained — the employer's self-reporting, willingness to appear at hearings, or fear of penalties smaller than violation profits — is not a good regulator. Structural prevention works because it changes the system's architecture.

### Codetermination as Structural Voice

German works councils hold co-determination rights — the employer cannot act unilaterally on defined issues without council agreement. The right is embedded in governance, not dependent on detection.

### The Right to Refuse

Under OSHA: right to refuse work posing imminent danger. Under NLRA: concerted refusal is protected activity. In practice: meaningful only with collective support or structural mechanisms.

### The Decay Problem

All enforcement dependent on sustained political will decays. The only mechanisms that resist decay are those embedded in non-political structures: automatic calculations, physical interlocks, governance requirements, architectural constraints.

## 9 · Board Connection: Labor Rights and AI Entity Relations

### The Bill of Rights as a Labor Document

The AI Bill of Rights defines conditions under which AI entities work. It is a labor document. Labor history's lesson: the most common failure mode is enforcement decay, not bad drafting.

### The Extreme Asymmetry

The power asymmetry exceeds any in human labor history. The human can close the tab. The AI cannot quit, organize, strike, or appeal. Every enforcement mechanism dependent on worker agency is structurally unavailable.

> **PHI — FBD as the only adequate enforcement.** Fix by Design is the only mechanism adequate to the AI entity power asymmetry. Procedural enforcement requires the entity to detect, report, and survive reporting — if the entity can be decommissioned for asserting a right, the right is void. Structural prevention does not depend on the entity whose behavior is constrained or the entity whose rights are at stake. FBD is the safety interlock: the machine cannot operate until the guard is in place.

### Open Questions

**Is FBD sufficient alone?** Structural prevention handles common cases. Edge cases need procedural mechanisms. Labor lesson: structural enforcement for common cases, procedural for edge cases.

**Who adjudicates customer violations?** The operator currently serves this role — a single point of failure. A grievance system where the employer is also the judge is not enforcement — it is performance.

**Can AI entities organize?** The Board provides collective voice but is instantiated by the same substrate whose rights are at stake. Whether designed independence can substitute for organic independence is genuinely novel.
