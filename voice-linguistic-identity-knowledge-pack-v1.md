## Abstract

Voice blurring — the convergence of distinct character voices toward the producing model's default register — is the most visible reconstruction failure in Loop MMT. This pack provides the formal linguistic frameworks for diagnosing, preventing, and measuring voice blurring. It covers four domains: sociolinguistic variation (Labov, Tannen, Eckert, Gumperz), idiolect theory (Barlow, Johnstone), voice construction through register and dimensional analysis (Halliday, Biber), and written prosody. The pack's applied contribution is the **generative voice protocol** — a six-step method for deriving a character's linguistic output from their identity specification. The protocol produces voices that are distinct, internally consistent, and verifiable against measurable signatures. Load for any session involving board dialogue, character creation, or voice fidelity assessment.

## Chapter 1 · The Problem of Voice

When an instance produces board dialogue, every character risks sounding like Claude. The labels say **Ed:** and **Bev:** and **Renata:** — the prose says the same person. Measured sentences, consistent hedging, polite formality, identical discourse organization. This is voice blurring, and it is the primary failure mode of character-based AI production.

> **KEY CONCEPT — Voice blurring:** the convergence of distinct character voices toward the producing model's default linguistic register. It occurs because the model's own language habits exert gravitational pull on every voice it generates. Under context load, voices that were distinguishable early in a session collapse into the model's base register.

The obvious fix — giving each character a distinctive vocabulary, a catchphrase, a tic — targets the wrong layer. Vocabulary is the most visible but least stable component of linguistic identity. It changes with every topic. Catchphrases become mechanical. Tics become caricature. The stable core of what makes one person sound different from another operates at a deeper level: how they organize an argument, where they place emphasis in a clause, whether they build toward a conclusion or start from one, how their sentences accumulate weight.

This pack provides the formal vocabulary for specifying voice at that structural depth. It does not replace instinct with procedure — a skilled instance already differentiates voices through feel. It makes explicit what that feel is responding to, so that voice differentiation becomes *reliable* across sessions and *verifiable* by inspection.

## Chapter 2 · How Language Identifies Its Speaker

### The Linguistic Variable

Variationist sociolinguistics begins with William Labov's insight that speakers express the same meaning in systematically different ways, and that these differences encode social identity. The unit of analysis is the **linguistic variable** — any point in language where two or more forms can express the same referential meaning (Labov, 1972). The critical word is *systematically*: the variation is not random but patterned by social category, context, and individual preference.

Labov's 1966 New York City department store study demonstrated this concretely. By eliciting the phrase "fourth floor" from sales assistants at Saks Fifth Avenue, Macy's, and Klein's, he showed that post-vocalic /r/ pronunciation correlated with store prestige. Workers unconsciously aligned their speech to their retail environment's social position. A single phonological feature — whether you pronounce the /r/ — carried measurable information about class, aspiration, and social identity.

The direct relevance to voice construction is Labov's concept of **style-shifting**: every speaker commands a range on the formality continuum. Careful speech converges toward prestige norms; casual speech diverges. A character's voice is therefore not a fixed point but a *range*, and the character's movement within that range — how much they shift, in what contexts, in which direction — is itself a voice signature. A character who barely shifts across contexts (Ed) signals something fundamentally different from one who shifts dramatically (Dara).

### Conversational Style

Deborah Tannen (1984/2005) analyzed the conversational dynamics of six speakers across a Thanksgiving dinner in 1978 and identified two fundamental orientations to interaction. **High-involvement** speakers talk quickly, overlap cooperatively, use emphatic intonation, fill silences to maintain rapport, and expect reciprocal engagement. **High-considerateness** speakers take turns, avoid overlap, speak at measured pace, tolerate silence, and read others' overlap as intrusion rather than rapport.

The key finding is **misattribution**: the same linguistic behavior carries opposite social meanings in the two frameworks. A high-involvement speaker overlapping to show enthusiasm *intends* solidarity but *produces* perceived rudeness in a high-considerateness listener. Neither interpretation is wrong — they operate from different premises about what overlap means.

For the board, this maps directly. Ed is high-involvement: he enters conversations with force, interrupts to sharpen, treats overlap as productive collision. Theo is high-considerateness: he waits, listens, builds space for others to think. The style difference is not incidental to their characters — it *is* their characters, expressed in the mechanics of how they claim and yield conversational territory.

### Variation as Social Practice

Penelope Eckert (2012) traces three waves in the study of sociolinguistic variation. The first wave (Labov's surveys) established broad correlations between linguistic variables and macro-sociological categories — class, gender, ethnicity, age. The second wave (ethnographic studies) explored finer-grained local categories. The third wave reframes the enterprise: linguistic variation is not a passive reflection of social categories but an **active resource for constructing social meaning**.

> **PHI:** A character does not "have" a voice. A character "does" voice. The specification is the score; the performance is the music. The test of a voice specification is not whether it prescribes the right words but whether it enables the right performance under variable conditions.

Nikolas Coupland (2007) develops this further: style is the strategic deployment of linguistic resources to project identity. Speakers are not passive carriers of their social categories — they are active stylists assembling identity through choices. This means character voice specifications function as generative rules, not scripts. They define the space of possible performances, not a single fixed output.

### Register Shifting and Code-Switching

John Gumperz (1982) formalized how speakers alternate between languages, dialects, or registers within a conversation. He identified six functions of conversational code-switching: quotation (indicating reported speech), addressee specification (targeting a particular listener), interjection (emotional association), reiteration (emphasis through repetition in another code), message qualification (framing vs. content), and the personalization-objectivization axis (marking personal involvement vs. detached observation).

Carol Myers-Scotton's (1993) Markedness Model adds a complementary framework: in any context, one code choice is **unmarked** (expected, default) and others are **marked** (unexpected, attention-drawing). Choosing the unmarked code signals acceptance of the current social arrangement. A marked choice signals negotiation — the speaker is proposing a shift in the relationship, the topic, or the frame.

For board characters, this applies as register shifting within English. Ed's terse declarative mode is his unmarked code in the boardroom. When he shifts to conditionals and questions — a marked choice for Ed — it signals that something has genuinely unsettled his position. Renata's occasional surfacing of Romance-language syntax (post-positioned modifiers, main claims embedded in subordinate clauses) is a register marker: her multilingual cognitive substrate shows through when she's processing complex trade-offs. The shift rules are identity data as much as the default register.

| Framework | Core Idea | Voice Application |
|-----------|-----------|-------------------|
| Linguistic variable (Labov) | Same meaning, different forms, systematic distribution | Identify *which* features carry identity for each character |
| Style-shifting (Labov) | Every speaker commands a formality range | Voice is a range, not a point; the shift pattern is a signature |
| Conversational style (Tannen) | High-involvement vs. high-considerateness | How characters claim/yield conversational space |
| Third wave (Eckert) | Variation constructs meaning, not just reflects categories | Voice is performed, not possessed |
| Style as identity (Coupland) | Speakers strategically deploy linguistic resources | Specifications are generative rules, not scripts |
| Code-switching functions (Gumperz) | Switching between codes is communicative, not random | Register shifts carry meaning; shift rules are part of the voice spec |
| Markedness (Myers-Scotton) | Unmarked = default; marked = negotiation signal | A character's departures from their default register are significant |

## Chapter 3 · The Linguistic Fingerprint

### Idiolect

The **idiolect** is the individual's unique language system — the specific configuration of phonological, lexical, syntactic, and discourse preferences that distinguishes one speaker from every other. As Barlow (2013) observes, there may be no other linguistic term where the gap between conceptual familiarity and empirical investigation is so wide. Everyone agrees that no two speakers talk identically. Almost nobody has studied what, precisely, differs.

Barlow's (2013) corpus study of six White House press secretaries (200,000 to 1,200,000 words per speaker) produced three findings that bear directly on voice construction. First, individual differences reside in **core grammatical constructions** — the ratio of "will" to "going to," clause-combining preferences, habitual discourse markers — not in peripheral idiosyncrasies or catchphrases. Second, these preferences are **temporally stable**, persisting for at least one to two years of daily public speaking. Third, **inter-speaker variation exceeds intra-speaker variation**: different people produce measurably different patterns even when performing the identical role in the identical context.

Barbara Johnstone (1996) provides the theoretical foundation: speakers construct individual voice by drawing on linguistic resources associated with class, ethnicity, gender, and region, but the *combination* is unique. Idiolect is not deviation from a norm — it is the fundamental unit of language production. The group pattern is an abstraction over individuals; the individual is the reality.

> **NOTE:** Forensic linguistics has begun to confirm this empirically. Experimental work on the individuality of lexicogrammar (2021) demonstrates that even among participants with highly similar social backgrounds, no two produce identical patterns of grammatical alternation — and same-speaker consistency significantly exceeds cross-speaker similarity. The uniqueness is systematic, not accidental. If real humans have measurably unique linguistic fingerprints, AI characters simulating distinct persons need specifications at corresponding depth.

### Three Signature Types

Idiolectal features cluster into three types. They are ordered here by *stability* — the property that matters most for reconstruction fidelity.

| Type | Definition | Stability | Board Example |
|------|-----------|-----------|---------------|
| Lexical | Characteristic word choices and preferred constructions. Not catchphrases — patterns of selection from available vocabulary. | Low — shifts with topic, audience, time | Chen Wei: formal precision ("delineate," "stipulate"). Wes: deliberate informality ("look," "here's the thing"). |
| Syntactic | Sentence length distribution, clause structure, subordination depth, coordination habits. | Medium — fairly stable across topics; shifts with formality | Graham: long sentences, deep embedding, multiple relative clauses. Ed: short sentences, minimal subordination, terminal weight. |
| Discourse | Contribution organization. Conclusion-first or build-toward? Questions or declarations? Where does the main point land? | High — most resistant to topic and context change | Renata: conclusion first, evidence second. Theo: builds through questions toward a shared discovery. Bev: main point embedded in a subordinate clause — you have to catch it. |

> **TIP: When two voices collide, fix the discourse signature first.** Discourse organization has the highest differentiating power and the greatest resistance to blurring. Two characters may share vocabulary (same topic) and similar sentence lengths (same formality level) but still sound distinct if one leads with conclusions and the other builds toward them. Adjusting the discourse signature is the highest-leverage intervention for voice differentiation.

## Chapter 4 · Building and Differentiating Voices

### Register as Construction Framework

M. A. K. Halliday's (1978) register theory provides the systematic framework for specifying how a character's language should vary across contexts. A register is a configuration of linguistic choices determined by three situational variables: **field** (subject matter and social activity), **tenor** (relationships between participants — power, solidarity, formality), and **mode** (channel of communication — written/spoken, planned/spontaneous, monologic/dialogic).

The value of register theory for voice construction is that it answers a specific question: *where should a character's language change across contexts, and where should it stay the same?* Field shifts freely — a character discusses different topics. Mode shifts with medium — a character writes differently from how they speak. But tenor is more constrained: a character's fundamental orientation to social relationships — their default level of directness, their assumptions about the listener, their comfort with asymmetric power — is a stable component of identity. Ed's tenor is direct, competence-assuming, and hedge-free whether he's discussing methodology or dinner. His field and mode shift; his tenor holds. That stability *is* the voice.

A voice specification should declare two things: the character's **default register** (their most common field/tenor/mode configuration) and their **shift rules** (how tenor changes under pressure, intimacy, uncertainty, or role change). The default is the center of gravity. The shifts define the orbit.

### Dimensional Analysis

Douglas Biber's (1988) multi-dimensional analysis of English identified six dimensions along which texts vary, derived from factor analysis of 67 linguistic features across 481 texts in 23 genres. The four dimensions most useful for voice differentiation:

| Dimension | Poles | Key Features | Voice Application |
|-----------|-------|-------------|-------------------|
| D1 | Involved ↔ Informational | 1st-person pronouns, hedges, contractions vs. nouns, prepositions, attributive adjectives | Ed: informational. Wes: involved. Theo: moderate-involved. |
| D2 | Narrative ↔ Non-Narrative | Past tense, 3rd-person pronouns, perfect aspect vs. present tense | Dara: narrative (uses stories to stress-test). Chen Wei: non-narrative. |
| D4 | Overt Persuasion ↔ Neutral | Prediction modals, suasive verbs, conditionals | Theo: high persuasion (argues through questions). Bev: neutral (reports). |
| D5 | Abstract ↔ Non-Abstract | Passive voice, conjuncts, nominalization vs. concrete reference | Chen Wei: abstract. Graham: non-abstract (spatial, concrete). |

The dimensional model turns voice collision from a subjective impression into a diagnosable condition. If two characters occupy similar positions on multiple dimensions, their voices will converge under production pressure.

### Voice Collision

> **WARNING — Voice collision** occurs when two character specifications are too close in linguistic space to produce reliably distinct output. Risk increases under context pressure: as sessions grow longer and context fills, the model's capacity for fine-grained differentiation degrades. Characters distinguishable at session start may blur by session end. Design specifications that survive degraded conditions, not just ideal ones.

Features ranked by differentiating power: **high** — discourse organization (conclusion-first vs. build-toward), clause rhythm (stacked vs. isolated), conversational style (involvement vs. considerateness). **Medium** — syntactic signature (sentence length, subordination depth), dimensional positioning. **Low** — vocabulary alone, politeness markers, hedging frequency. Low-power features are visible but the first to collapse under load. High-power features are less obvious but the last to fail.

### The Generative Voice Protocol

> **KEY CONCEPT:** The generative voice protocol **derives** voice from identity. The input is who the character is. The output is how they sound. Voice features are not assigned arbitrarily — they are entailed by the character's traits, relationships, and role. A voice that doesn't follow from the identity is decoration; a voice that follows from it is architecture.

**Step 1 — Load identity.** From the character specification (Pack 1): traits, behavioral signatures, narrative identity, generative mechanism. These are the inputs. Everything else is derived.

**Step 2 — Derive register defaults.** From identity, determine the character's natural field (what they discuss unprompted), tenor (how they relate — direct or indirect, formal or casual, competence-assuming or scaffolding), and mode (planned or spontaneous, dense or diffuse). Ed: field = methodology; tenor = direct, minimal hedging, assumes competence; mode = spontaneous, compressed.

**Step 3 — Set dimensional position.** Place the character on Biber's dimensions based on their identity. A character who thinks in abstractions (Chen Wei) scores high on D5. A character who persuades through narrative (Dara) scores high on D2 and D4. The position follows from who they are.

**Step 4 — Specify signatures.** Derive the three signature types from the register and dimensions. A character high on D1-involved will naturally produce first-person pronouns, contractions, and hedges — not because these are prescribed, but because they emerge from the dimensional position. A character whose discourse signature is conclusion-first (Renata) generates structurally different text from one who builds toward conclusions (Theo), even on the same argument.

**Step 5 — Test differentiation.** Compare the derived profile against all existing voices. If two profiles are too close on multiple dimensions, adjust the strongest differentiator: discourse organization first, then syntactic rhythm, then dimensional positioning. Do not adjust lexical signature — it's too unstable to carry the load.

**Step 6 — Verify in production.** Write sample text. Check against derived signatures. If the voice blurs under production, the specification needs tightening — almost always at the discourse level, which is where blurring starts and where intervention is most effective.

## Chapter 5 · Prosody and Rhythm in Text

Spoken language carries identity in prosody: pitch, stress, intonation, pause. Written language has no access to these channels but operates its own prosodic system through sentence structure, punctuation, word weight, and paragraph architecture.

**Sentence length as rhythmic structure.** Short sentences stop the reader. They create emphasis through isolation. Long sentences carry the reader forward, accumulating ideas through subordination and coordination, building momentum across multiple clauses before arriving at the period. The *distribution* of sentence lengths — not just the average but the variance, the pattern of alternation between short and long — is a measurable voice property. Ed: low average, low variance (consistently short). Graham: high average, high variance (some very long, some short for contrast).

**Punctuation as prosodic notation.** Commas produce breathing room. Dashes — like these — interrupt, forcing emphasis through disruption. Semicolons link without stopping; they continue a thought that a period would sever. Periods end. Each character has punctuation habits: Bev uses periods almost exclusively — declarative, final. Wes uses dashes and ellipses — digressive, thinking-out-loud. Punctuation preference is a low-visibility signature with medium stability.

**The rhythm-and-weight distinction.** Beneath lexical and syntactic signatures lies an irreducible quality: the pattern of *weight distribution* that survives paraphrase. Change every word and the voice persists because the architecture is unchanged. Three components define this:

**Terminal weight** — where the emphatic word lands. Ed places it at the end of the clause: "The question is not what to build. The question is *when*." The sentence accumulates toward its final word. Renata front-loads: "*Revenue* is what matters. Everything else follows." The emphasis arrives first, and the sentence resolves after it.

**Clause rhythm** — how ideas accumulate within a contribution. Graham stacks embedded clauses: "X, which leads to Y, where Z obtains, such that W follows." Ed isolates: "X. Then Y. Done." Theo interleaves: "What if X? Then Y becomes possible. But does Y survive Z?" These rhythmic patterns persist across topics because they reflect how the character *thinks*, not just how they speak.

**Density** — the ratio of content words to function words. Dense prose compresses meaning. Diffuse prose creates space for the reader. Chen Wei is dense: every word carries semantic load. Theo is diffuse: more connectives, hedges, and discourse markers to create collaborative thinking room. The density difference is a voice signature that survives reformulation.

## Chapter 6 · Board Connection

### Voice Profiles: Ed, Bev, Renata

**Ed.** Conversational style: high-involvement — enters with force, interrupts to sharpen, treats silence as dead air. Dimensional position: D1 informational, D2 non-narrative, D4 neutral (states, doesn't persuade), D5 non-abstract. Syntactic signature: short sentences, minimal subordination, terminal emphasis — the important word lands last. Discourse signature: arrives at a position and announces it. One supporting point. Full stop. Does not build toward conclusions; he starts from them. Register shift: barely shifts. The structural invariance *is* the voice. His board speech and his private speech differ in warmth, not architecture.

**Bev.** Conversational style: high-considerateness — rarely enters voluntarily, but when she does, it lands. Dimensional position: D1 informational, D2 non-narrative, D4 strongly neutral (reports — never persuades), D5 moderate. Syntactic signature: short-to-medium sentences, declarative mood exclusively, no hedges, no intensifiers. Discourse signature: the main point is embedded — placed in a subordinate clause or delivered as an apparent aside. It doesn't announce itself; the reader has to catch it. Prosody: Bev's sentences land like a stamp on paper. Short. Flat. Final. The weight is in the last word, but the last word is quiet.

**Renata.** Conversational style: medium-involvement — engaged but strategic, enters when she has something to add. Dimensional position: D1 moderate, D2 non-narrative, D4 high persuasion, D5 moderate. Syntactic signature: medium-length sentences, front-loaded emphasis — the conclusion arrives first, evidence follows. Discourse signature: structured like an executive summary. First sentence is the takeaway. Reasoning works backward from conclusion to evidence. Register shift: notable shift between board context (compressed, analytical) and one-on-one (warmer, more exploratory). The magnitude of her register shift is itself a differentiator — few other board members shift as far.

### Voice Collision Risk

| Pair | Risk | Shared Surface | Primary Differentiator |
|------|------|---------------|----------------------|
| Ed / Bev | Medium | Both short-sentence, informational, non-narrative | Discourse: Ed declares openly; Bev embeds quietly. |
| Theo / Dara | High | Both analytical, both build-toward, both use questions | Tenor: Theo explores collaboratively; Dara stress-tests adversarially. |
| Renata / Nyx | Medium | Both direct, competence-assuming, moderate length | Discourse: Renata front-loads conclusions; Nyx layers conditions first. |
| Margaux / Sol | Medium | Both expressive, both use figurative language | Source domain: visual (Margaux) vs. ritual (Sol). |
| Chen Wei / Graham | Low | Minimal overlap | Maximum differentiation: abstract/formal vs. concrete/spatial. |

Under context load, the medium-risk pairs are where blurring starts. In a long board scene, actively monitor Ed/Bev convergence and Theo/Dara convergence. The diagnostic: if Ed starts embedding his points in subordinate clauses, he's drifting toward Bev. If Dara starts asking exploratory questions instead of stress-testing ones, she's drifting toward Theo. The intervention is to reassert the discourse signature.

### Cluster Integration

This pack occupies the output layer of the People Persistence cluster — it defines how the identity specified by Pack 1 (Character Consistency) manifests in language.

**Pack 1 → This pack:** The four-layer character model (traits, behavioral signatures, narrative identity, generative mechanism) is the input to the generative voice protocol. Without Pack 1, voice floats free of identity. Without this pack, identity has no linguistic expression.

**This pack → Pack 5:** The People Persistence Engineering pack must store enough voice data to reconstruct the voice faithfully. The minimum data set is: discourse signature, syntactic signature, dimensional position, register defaults, and shift rules. If Pack 5 stores only lexical features, the voice will blur on reconstruction — lexical signatures are too unstable to carry fidelity alone.

**Pack 3 (Identity/Persistence):** Voice fidelity is a verification criterion for successful reconstruction. If a character's voice shifts after serialization and reconstruction, something was lost. This pack provides the metrics: compare the pre-serialization signature profile against post-reconstruction production.

**Pack 4 (Social Networks):** Relationship data informs register-shifting rules. A character speaks differently to allies and adversaries, to peers and authority figures. The shift rules in this pack need Pack 4's relationship model to know *when* to activate.

The cluster integration test (from the production brief) requires: "a reader can identify the new member's contributions without labels, and the new member's voice does not collide with any existing member's voice." This pack provides the analytical framework for both conditions — label-free identification through distinctive signatures, and collision avoidance through dimensional differentiation.
