About This Document
:::

Reference on rhetoric and argumentation for AI advisory work. Covers classical persuasion, the Toulmin model, stasis theory, argumentation schemes, evidence types, fallacies, Perelman's audience theory, and pragma-dialectics. The intended use case: equipping AI advisors to help human operators structure arguments, marshal evidence, defend positions, and detect reasoning failures — the skills underlying position papers, white papers, and any document that must persuade through reason. Sources: Aristotle, Toulmin (1958), Perelman & Olbrechts-Tyteca (1958/1969), van Eemeren & Grootendorst (1984–2004), Walton, Reed & Macagno (2008), Hamblin (1970).
:::

::: {#ch-classical .chapter}
::: chapter-number
Section 1
:::

::: chapter-title
Classical Foundations
:::

Aristotle's *Rhetoric*, composed across two periods at the Academy and the Lyceum (roughly 367–322 BCE), remains the foundational text. It established the vocabulary and structural categories that every subsequent theory builds on or reacts against.

::: {.section}
::: section-title
Three Modes of Persuasion
:::

Aristotle identified three **technical means of persuasion** — means created by the speaker through the speech itself, distinguished from pre-existing evidence like witnesses, contracts, or oaths.

**Ethos** — persuasion through the speaker's character. The audience's willingness to engage with an argument depends on whether they perceive the speaker as credible, competent, and acting in good faith. Aristotle placed ethos first. Credibility is the gate through which every other appeal must pass.

**Pathos** — persuasion through the audience's emotional and psychological state. Aristotle observed that humans judge differently depending on their emotional condition. This is not a license for manipulation; it's an observation that ignoring the audience's emotional landscape means failing to communicate. Understanding what the audience values and fears is prerequisite to reaching them.

**Logos** — persuasion through the argument's own structure and evidence. Aristotle identified two logical forms: the **enthymeme** (a deductive argument from probable premises, typically with one premise left unstated because the audience already accepts it) and **argument from example** (inductive reasoning — particular cases marshaled to support a general claim).

::: {.callout .key}
::: callout-label
The Enthymeme
:::

The enthymeme bridges formal logic and everyday argument. Unlike a full syllogism, it relies on shared assumptions the audience already holds rather than spelling out every premise. When Toulmin later formalized the "warrant" — the unstated reasoning linking evidence to claim — he was describing what Aristotle identified twenty-three centuries earlier. The enthymeme is the structural ancestor of the implicit warrant.
:::

Two additional concepts round out the classical picture: **kairos** (the timeliness and appropriateness of the argument to its setting — the right argument at the wrong time fails) and **telos** (the purpose of the speech, which governs how ethos, pathos, and logos should be balanced).
:::

::: {.section}
::: section-title
Three Genres of Oratory
:::

| Genre | Time | Judgment | Modern Analogue |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Deliberative** | Future | What should we do? | Policy proposals, strategic plans, recommendations |
| **Forensic** | Past | What happened? Was it just? | Post-mortems, incident reports, legal argument |
| **Epideictic** | Present | What do we value? | Mission statements, culture docs, praise/blame |

Aristotle treated epideictic as the least important genre. Perelman (Section 7) later reversed that judgment, arguing that shared values underpin all argumentation and that epideictic rhetoric — the rhetoric of values — is therefore foundational, not decorative.
:::

::: {.section}
::: section-title
Five Canons of Rhetoric
:::

Roman rhetoricians, principally Cicero (106–43 BCE) and Quintilian (c. 35–100 CE), organized rhetoric into five operations: **Invention** (finding arguments), **Arrangement** (organizing them), **Style** (choosing language), **Memory** (retaining the speech), and **Delivery** (presenting it). For written argumentation — the primary concern of this pack — invention and arrangement do most of the work. Stasis theory (Section 3) is the classical tool of invention; the Toulmin model (Section 2) serves as a modern tool of arrangement.
:::
:::

::: {#ch-toulmin .chapter}
::: chapter-number
Section 2
:::

::: chapter-title
Argument Structure: The Toulmin Model
:::

Stephen Toulmin (1922–2009) published *The Uses of Argumentation* in 1958, proposing an alternative to the classical syllogism for analyzing how arguments actually work in practice. His model was originally intended for legal reasoning; Toulmin himself did not anticipate its broad applicability until after scholars in rhetoric and communication adopted it.

::: {.section}
::: section-title
Six Components
:::

**Claim** — the assertion to be proved. Must be disputable. If no one would disagree, there is nothing to argue.

**Grounds** — the evidence, facts, and data that support the claim. This is the foundation — the raw material of persuasion.

**Warrant** — the reasoning that connects grounds to claim. It answers: "Why does this evidence mean this claim is true?" Warrants are frequently implicit; the audience is expected to supply them from shared assumptions. An unstated warrant is structurally identical to the missing premise in an enthymeme.

**Backing** — additional support for the warrant. If the audience might question the reasoning link itself (not the evidence, but why the evidence is relevant), backing provides that justification. Backing supports the warrant; grounds support the claim. They are not interchangeable.

**Qualifier** — words limiting scope and certainty: "probably," "usually," "in most cases." Absolute claims are trivially easy to defeat — one counterexample sinks them. Qualified claims account for exceptions upfront and are therefore harder to refute. Qualifiers are the difference between an argument that sounds strong and one that actually is.

**Rebuttal** — conditions under which the claim fails, or counterarguments the arguer acknowledges. Pre-empting the opposition demonstrates intellectual honesty and makes the argument more resilient.

::: {.callout .key}
::: callout-label
Primary and Secondary Triads
:::

**Claim + Grounds + Warrant** form the essential core. An argument missing any of these is structurally incomplete. **Backing + Qualifier + Rebuttal** strengthen but are not always required. In contested domains — which is to say, in every domain where a position paper matters — you need all six.
:::
:::

::: {.section}
::: section-title
Argument vs. Explanation
:::

A frequent confusion: an **argument** tries to establish that a claim is true. An **explanation** tries to show why something already accepted is the case. The distinction matters because it determines what work the text must do. If you're explaining to an audience that already agrees, evidence serves illustration. If you're arguing to an audience that doesn't yet agree, evidence carries the burden of proof. The same sentence can function as either depending on whether the audience accepts the conclusion.
:::

::: {.section}
::: section-title
Using the Model
:::

The Toulmin model works for both construction and analysis. To **build** an argument: (1) state a specific, disputable claim; (2) gather credible, relevant, sufficient grounds; (3) articulate the warrant explicitly; (4) add backing if the warrant might be questioned; (5) qualify the claim to match what evidence supports; (6) identify the strongest counterarguments and build rebuttals. To **analyze** an argument: map it onto the six components. Whatever is missing or weak is the vulnerability.
:::
:::

::: {#ch-stasis .chapter}
::: chapter-number
Section 3
:::

::: chapter-title
Finding the Dispute: Stasis Theory
:::

People routinely talk past each other because they're arguing at different levels — one person disputes the facts while the other disputes the policy. Before you can argue effectively, you need to locate where the disagreement actually lives.

Stasis theory, developed by **Hermagoras of Temnos** (Greek rhetorician, variously dated to the 2nd or 1st century BCE) and refined by **Cicero** and **Quintilian**, provides a systematic method. The word *stasis* (Greek: "standstill") names the point where opposing positions meet and cannot advance until the dispute is resolved.

Four questions, asked in order — each depends on the answer to the preceding one:

| Stasis | Question | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Conjecture** | Did it happen? Does it exist? | Did the system actually fail, or was this expected behavior? |
| **Definition** | What should we call it? | Is this a "bug" or a "design limitation"? |
| **Quality** | Is it good or bad? Justified? | Was the trade-off acceptable given the constraints? |
| **Policy** | Who decides? What should be done? | Should the operator or the stakeholder make this call? |

::: {.callout .tip}
::: callout-label
Why This Matters
:::

When a disagreement stalls, run the stasis questions. If you're arguing about what to do (policy) but haven't agreed on what happened (conjecture), the policy argument is premature. Stasis forces you to resolve disputes in the right order. It also reveals when two parties think they disagree but actually agree on three of four levels — in which case the apparent disagreement is narrower than it feels.
:::
:::

::: {#ch-schemes .chapter}
::: chapter-number
Section 4
:::

::: chapter-title
Argumentation Schemes
:::

An argumentation scheme is a stereotypical pattern of reasoning — a named template that captures how a common type of argument works. The concept descends from Aristotle's *topoi* and was developed most comprehensively by **Douglas Walton**, **Chris Reed**, and **Fabrizio Macagno** in *Argumentation Schemes* (Cambridge, 2008). Their compendium catalogs sixty-five main schemes, with subtypes bringing the count to roughly ninety-six.

Each scheme has three components: a **named pattern**, a **premise-conclusion structure**, and a set of **critical questions** — defeasibility conditions that test whether the argument holds. The critical questions are the key innovation: they define what you'd need to show to defeat the argument.

::: {.section}
::: section-title
Major Families
:::

**Source-based**: argument from expert opinion, witness testimony, position to know, popular opinion. Critical questions probe the source's actual expertise, potential bias, and whether qualified experts disagree.

**Case-based**: argument from example, analogy, precedent. Critical questions ask whether the cases are genuinely similar in relevant respects and whether important differences have been suppressed.

**Causal**: argument from cause to effect, correlation to cause, sign. Critical questions probe whether the causal mechanism is established, whether alternative causes exist, and whether correlation might be coincidental.

**Practical reasoning**: I have goal G; action A achieves G; therefore I should do A. Critical questions probe side effects, alternative means, and whether the goal is actually desirable.

**Character-based**: ad hominem (abusive, circumstantial, tu quoque), argument from bias, poisoning the well. These attack the person rather than the argument.
:::

::: {.callout .key}
::: callout-label
Schemes and Fallacies
:::

C.L. Hamblin's *Fallacies* (1970) demonstrated that many traditional "fallacies" are not inherently invalid. An appeal to expert opinion is perfectly reasonable when the expert is genuinely qualified, independent, and speaking within their competence. The same pattern becomes fallacious when the "expert" has no relevant expertise or a conflict of interest. Schemes are not good or bad — they are patterns that can be used well or badly. The critical questions for each scheme mark the boundary between legitimate use and fallacious misuse.
:::
:::

::: chapter-number
Section 5

::: chapter-title
Evidence
:::

Evidence fills the "grounds" slot of the Toulmin model. The type of evidence you use, and how you combine types, determines whether the argument holds weight with the audience.

| Type | What It Is | Strength | Risk |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Statistical** | Numbers, data, percentages, measurements | Highest formal weight | Misleading through selection, framing, or flawed method |
| **Testimonial** | Expert opinion, authority statements | High when credible | Entirely dependent on the source's competence and independence |
| **Empirical** | Direct observation, experiment | High — first-hand | Limited scope; observer bias |
| **Historical** | Precedents, past events | Moderate — grounded in real outcomes | Past conditions may not match present |
| **Anecdotal** | Personal stories, individual cases | Low alone; high for engagement | Cherry-picking; not generalizable |
| **Analogical** | Comparison to similar situations | Lowest standalone | Analogy may break on closer examination |

Strong arguments layer multiple types. Data establishes the pattern; expert testimony lends authority; anecdote makes the pattern concrete and memorable. Each type compensates for the others' weaknesses.

::: {.section}
::: section-title
Burden of Proof
:::

Whoever makes a claim bears the burden of supporting it. In formal debate, the affirmative side carries the burden. In legal contexts, the standard varies: "beyond reasonable doubt" in criminal cases, "preponderance of the evidence" in civil ones. In informal argument, the burden is effectively shared — both sides need to support their positions or neither has reason to be taken seriously. An unsupported assertion creates no obligation on the audience to disprove it.
:::
:::

::: {#ch-fallacies .chapter}
::: chapter-number
Section 6
:::

::: chapter-title
Fallacies
:::

A fallacy is a reasoning error — specifically, accepting or rejecting a claim on grounds that don't actually warrant it. **Formal fallacies** are errors in logical structure (the argument is invalid regardless of content). **Informal fallacies** are errors in content, relevance, or premises despite possibly valid form. Informal fallacies are far more common in practice and far harder to spot.

::: {.callout .warn}
::: callout-label
Warning
:::

Naming a fallacy does not refute an argument. The **fallacy fallacy** assumes that a poorly argued conclusion must be false. It may be true — just badly supported. And many named fallacies have legitimate counterparts (Section 4). Label carefully.
:::

::: {.section}
::: section-title
Fallacies of Relevance
:::

The premises are psychologically connected to the conclusion but logically irrelevant.

**Ad hominem** — attacking the person, not the argument. Subtypes: abusive (insult), circumstantial (alleging bias from self-interest), tu quoque ("you do it too"). A person's character is not evidence for or against their argument's truth.

**Straw man** — distorting an opponent's position to make it easier to attack. Defeating the distortion leaves the real argument untouched.

**Red herring** — introducing an irrelevant topic to divert attention. Diagnostic: remove the supposed red herring from the discussion. If the main argument is unaffected, it was irrelevant.

**Appeal to emotion** — substituting emotional response for evidence. Emotions legitimately inform value judgments but cannot replace evidence about facts.

**Appeal to popularity** — claiming something is true because many believe it. Popularity has no bearing on truth.
:::

::: {.section}
::: section-title
Fallacies of Presumption
:::

The argument smuggles in unjustified assumptions.

**False dilemma** — presenting two options when more exist.

**Begging the question** — the conclusion is hidden in the premises. The argument is circular.

**False cause** — assuming causation from correlation (cum hoc) or from temporal sequence (post hoc). B following A does not establish A caused B.

**Hasty generalization** — broad conclusion from insufficient evidence.

**Slippery slope** — claiming one step inevitably leads to extreme consequences without establishing the causal chain.

**Appeal to ignorance** — claiming something is true because it hasn't been proven false, or vice versa.
:::

::: {.section}
::: section-title
Fallacies of Ambiguity
:::

**Equivocation** — using a word in two senses within the same argument.

**Composition/Division** — assuming what's true of parts is true of the whole, or vice versa.
:::

::: {.callout .note}
::: callout-label
Note
:::

The taxonomy of fallacies is genuinely disputed among logicians. Multiple competing classification systems exist, and the boundaries between categories are fuzzy. This section presents a working taxonomy organized by function — relevance, presumption, ambiguity — not a definitive ontology.
:::
:::

::: chapter-title
Audience and Adherence: Perelman's New Rhetoric
:::

In 1958 — the same year Toulmin published his model — Chaïm Perelman (1912–1984) and Lucie Olbrechts-Tyteca (1899–1987) published *The New Rhetoric: A Treatise on Argumentation*. Where Toulmin analyzed argument structure, Perelman analyzed the relationship between argument and audience. The work was a deliberate challenge to three centuries of Western philosophy's preference for self-evidence and formal proof as the only legitimate forms of rational argument.

::: {.section}
::: section-title
Core Concepts
:::

**Adherence** is the goal — gaining the audience's genuine assent to a thesis, not mere acknowledgment but acceptance that disposes toward action.

**Universal audience**: an idealized construct of all reasonable people. Arguments aimed at the universal audience aspire to be *convincing* — they rely on premises any rational person would accept. **Particular audience**: the actual people being addressed. Arguments aimed at particular audiences are *persuasive* — they rely on premises this specific group holds.

**Values are central.** Perelman reversed Aristotle's ranking of epideictic rhetoric. Where Aristotle treated the rhetoric of values as least important, Perelman argued it's foundational: every argument rests on shared values between speaker and audience. If the audience doesn't hold the value that makes your warrant work, no evidence will move them.

**Liaison** (association) connects ideas — analogy, example, model, metaphor — transferring adherence from accepted premises to new conclusions. **Dissociation** breaks apart concepts that seem unified (appearance vs. reality, letter vs. spirit), allowing you to accept the opponent's facts while rejecting their interpretation.
:::

::: {.callout .key}
::: callout-label
Why This Matters
:::

A structurally sound argument (Toulmin), correctly aimed at the real dispute (stasis), using legitimate schemes and strong evidence, will still fail if aimed at the wrong audience or grounded in values the audience doesn't share. Arguments don't exist in isolation. They exist in the space between speaker and audience. Perelman's contribution is the formalization of that space.
:::
:::

::: {#ch-pragma .chapter}
::: chapter-number
Section 8
:::

::: chapter-title
Reasonable Discussion: Pragma-Dialectics
:::

Pragma-dialectics, developed by **Frans van Eemeren** and **Rob Grootendorst** (d. 2000) at the University of Amsterdam from the late 1970s onward, models how argumentative exchanges should work when the goal is reasonable resolution of disagreement rather than victory.

::: {.section}
::: section-title
Four Stages of Critical Discussion
:::

| Stage | Purpose | What Happens |
|---|---|---|
| **Confrontation** | Identify the dispute | Both parties verbalize what they agree and disagree about. |
| **Opening** | Set ground rules | Parties agree on starting points — what counts as evidence, what rules govern. |
| **Argumentation** | Exchange arguments | The protagonist defends the standpoint; the antagonist challenges it. |
| **Concluding** | Assess outcome | Parties determine whether and how the disagreement is resolved. |
:::

::: {.section}
::: section-title
Ten Rules and Fallacies
:::

Van Eemeren and Grootendorst proposed ten rules for critical discussion. Systematic violation of any rule constitutes a fallacy. Key rules include: parties must not prevent each other from advancing or questioning standpoints (freedom); whoever advances a standpoint must defend it if asked (burden of proof); attacks must address the standpoint actually advanced, not a distortion (standpoint); defense must use relevant argumentation (relevance); reasoning must be logically valid or validly applicable (logic); and conclusions must follow from the argumentation stage (concluding). The full set covers unexpressed premises, shared starting points, argument schemes, and procedural closure.
:::

::: {.section}
::: section-title
Strategic Maneuvering
:::

A later extension of the theory: real arguers try to be both reasonable and effective. Strategic maneuvering describes how they balance dialectical obligations (follow the rules) with rhetorical goals (win) by choosing favorable starting points, adapting to the audience, and exploiting presentational choices — all within the rules. When maneuvering crosses the line into rule violation, it becomes fallacious.
:::
:::

::: {#ch-synthesis .chapter}
::: chapter-number
Section 9
:::

::: chapter-title
Building a Position
:::

The frameworks compose into a workflow:

**1. Locate the dispute (Stasis).** Run the four questions. Is the disagreement about facts, definitions, values, or policy? Don't build at the wrong level.

**2. Know the audience (Perelman).** Who must be convinced? What do they accept? What values do they hold? Calibrate for the actual audience, not the audience you wish you had.

**3. Structure the argument (Toulmin).** State the claim. Identify the grounds. Articulate the warrant. Add backing. Qualify the claim to match the evidence. Build in rebuttals.

**4. Choose argument types (Schemes).** Which patterns fit? Analogy? Expert opinion? Consequences? For each scheme, run its critical questions. If the argument doesn't survive its own test, it won't survive the opponent's.

**5. Fill the grounds (Evidence).** Layer types: data for the pattern, testimony for authority, anecdote for concreteness.

**6. Self-audit (Fallacies).** Check for ad hominem, straw man, false dilemma, begging the question. The fallacy catalog is a checklist, not an accusation tool.

**7. Set procedural norms (Pragma-dialectics).** Especially in adversarial contexts: agree on what counts as evidence, where the burden of proof lies, and what resolution looks like.

::: {.callout .tip}
::: callout-label
The Charity Test
:::

Before finalizing: can you state the strongest version of the opposing argument? If not, you don't understand the dispute well enough to write about it. The principle of charity — reconstructing the opponent's case in its strongest possible form before responding — is not politeness. It's quality control. An argument that only defeats a weak version of the opposition has proved nothing.
:::

Board Connection

::: chapter-title
Board Connection
:::

**Toulmin → Fix by Design.** FBD enforces architectural soundness through structure, not convention. The Toulmin model does the same for arguments: a missing warrant is a structural defect regardless of how confident the speaker sounds — as code missing a validation handler is structurally broken regardless of whether inputs happen to be clean.

**Stasis → Talk-First Protocol.** Loop MMT scopes the problem before building. Stasis theory locates the actual dispute before arguing. Both prevent the same failure mode: constructing a well-built solution to the wrong problem.

**Critical Questions → Self-Review Protocol.** The self-review stress-tests every deliverable against known failure modes. Critical questions for argumentation schemes serve the same function — they are the test suite for arguments. An argument that fails its own critical questions is the rhetorical equivalent of code that fails its own tests.

**Universal Audience → Operator as Coherence Layer.** The board constructs arguments for an idealized rational audience. The operator calibrates those arguments for the actual stakeholders — Rick, Christine, Jamie. This is the Perelman problem: the gap between the universal and particular audience, bridged by someone who knows both.

**Pragma-Dialectics Stages → Session Structure.** Confrontation → opening → argumentation → concluding is structurally isomorphic to preflight → scope agreement → build → EOD handoff. Both impose procedural discipline on collaborative problem-solving. The isomorphism is structural, not accidental — both patterns exist because sequential, stage-gated resolution of disagreement converges faster than unstructured exchange.

**Fallacy Detection → Incident Reports.** When reasoning fails in Loop MMT, an incident report documents the failure mode and proposes structural prevention. Fallacy analysis is the same operation applied to argumentation: identify the specific type of reasoning failure, document the root cause, design a structural fix.
:::
