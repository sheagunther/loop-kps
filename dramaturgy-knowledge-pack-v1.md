## About This Document

Dramaturgy is the structural design of collaborative performance — how an experience is architected so that the people inside it can do their best work. This pack covers ensemble coherence, improvisation structure, status dynamics, scene architecture, and the audience-performer relationship. It draws on seven primary texts: Johnstone's *Impro*, Spolin's *Improvisation for the Theater*, Close and Halpern's *Truth in Comedy*, Bogart and Landau's *The Viewpoints Book*, Brecht's *Brecht on Theatre*, Brook's *The Empty Space*, and Boal's *Theatre of the Oppressed*. The pack is operationally focused: not a survey of theater history but a toolkit of structural principles that apply to ensemble collaborative work, including the Advisory Board's work within Loop MMT.

## Section 1 · Dramaturgy as Structural Design

The dramaturg does not write, does not direct, and does not perform. The dramaturg designs the architecture of the experience. Gotthold Ephraim Lessing formalized the role in *Hamburg Dramaturgy* (1767–1769) — one of the oldest named positions in modern theater. The dramaturg's job is to examine how a theatrical event works as a structure: where it builds, where it flags, where the audience's attention is held and where it drifts, what each scene does for the whole.

Ken Cerniglia, whose Broadway credits include *Hadestown* and *Peter and the Starcatcher*, describes the position: the dramaturg is "the suggester" — not the decider. The dramaturg brings structural analysis, historical research, and critical perspective, then hands them to the director and playwright to use as they see fit. The dramaturg's tools are questions, not answers.

> **KEY — The container principle**
> The dramaturg designs the container. The performers fill it. Structure is not content — it is the architecture within which content becomes possible. A well-designed container makes discovery structurally likely. A poorly designed one constrains what the performers can find. The quality of the container determines the ceiling of the work.

### Spatial Dramaturgy

The dramaturgical lens extends beyond theater. Holger Kleine (*The Drama of Space*, Birkhäuser, 2017) applies it to architecture: a sequence of rooms is a sequence of scenes. An entrance is an initiation. A corridor builds suspense. A sudden opening delivers a reveal. Every building has a script — people enter, pause, move, gather, leave — whether or not the architect shaped those actions intentionally. Spatial dramaturgy makes the shaping intentional.

## Section 2 · Ensemble Theory

### Coherence Without Central Control

An ensemble is a group that maintains coherence without one person controlling every moment. The director may shape the vision in rehearsal, but during performance the ensemble self-organizes — through shared vocabulary, practiced responsiveness, and mutual trust. In improvisation, where no script exists, ensemble coherence is the central problem and the central achievement.

Research on musical ensemble dynamics finds that group performance is one of the most cognitively complex tasks humans undertake, and that the dynamics within performing groups are not additive. Individual contributions do not sum linearly to a group outcome. What the ensemble produces emerges from interaction. Systems theory applies: the ensemble is a system with emergent properties, not a collection of individuals performing in parallel.

### Group Mind

"Group mind" in improvisation is the state where the ensemble functions as a unified intelligence without explicit coordination. No one decides the direction; everyone senses it. Del Close pursued this as the aspiration of the Harold form — the moment when the ensemble discovers connections no individual planned. Group mind is not mystical. It emerges from extended practice, from exercises that train attention to others' contributions, and from the willingness to subordinate individual cleverness to collective discovery.

### Offers and Acceptance

Keith Johnstone defines an **offer** as anything one performer does that another can respond to: a word, a gesture, a movement, a silence. The ensemble's fundamental skill is recognizing offers and building on them. **Blocking** — refusing or negating an offer — is what Johnstone calls "a form of aggression." It shuts down possibility. **Accepting** ("yes, and") opens possibility. But acceptance is not compliance. You can accept an offer while disagreeing, while redirecting, while playing high status. Acceptance means working with what's been given rather than against it.

### Ensemble Casting

Every ensemble member serves two roles simultaneously. They are a **character** — possessing a distinctive voice, perspective, and way of being. They are also a **function** — the skeptic, the dreamer, the pragmatist, the one who grounds, the one who provokes. The art of ensemble casting lies in aligning these dimensions: the skeptic is skeptical because that's who they are, and the ensemble was designed to include someone like that. When identity and function separate — when someone performs a role that doesn't match their nature — the ensemble feels false.

> **KEY — Dramatic function vs. personal identity**
> A well-cast ensemble member exists both as themselves and as what they do for the whole. The role emerges from the identity; the identity serves the role. Ensemble design succeeds when every member's natural inclination produces exactly what the collective needs.

## Section 3 · Status Transactions

Keith Johnstone's theory of status, developed at the Royal Court Theatre Studio beginning in 1963 and published in *Impro* (1979), is his most influential contribution. The central claim: status is something you *do*, not something you *are*. A person of low social standing can play high status. A powerful person can play low. Status is behavioral — communicated through body language, speech patterns, spatial behavior, and timing. It operates continuously, in every interaction, mostly below conscious awareness.

| Dimension | High Status | Low Status |
|-----------|------------|------------|
| **Head** | Still while speaking | Frequent movement, excessive nodding |
| **Body** | Smooth, deliberate movement | Jerky, twitchy, unnecessary movement |
| **Eyes** | Steady contact held | Quick, furtive glances |
| **Space** | Expands, takes up room | Contracts, minimizes footprint |
| **Speech** | Complete sentences, unhurried | Broken phrases, rushed |
| **Fillers** | Long pauses late in sentences ("wait for me") | Short fillers early in sentences ("feel free to interrupt") |

### The Seesaw and Status Flexibility

The **seesaw principle**: when one person's status rises, the other's tends to fall. But this is a tendency, not a law. The real skill is **status flexibility** — the ability to raise and lower at will, moment by moment, in service of the ensemble. Those who can shift fluidly between leading and following, between commanding attention and yielding space, are the most effective collaborators. Rigid status — always high or always low — limits both the individual and the group.

> **TIP — Ensemble status**
> "Within the team, your status will continuously change according to circumstances. To serve the ensemble, you will have to alternate between staying in the background and taking the initiative." (Imprology.com)

### Blocking, Accepting, and High-Status Acceptance

Johnstone found a structural link between status and spontaneity. High-status players tend to block: they refuse others' offers because accepting would mean ceding control. Low-status players tend to accept: they go along. Johnstone argues for a third possibility — **high-status acceptance**. You can be in charge and still say yes to others' inventions. Authority without rigidity. Leadership without blocking. This combination is what makes the most effective ensemble leaders, and it is vanishingly rare outside of trained performers.

## Section 4 · Structured Improvisation

### Viola Spolin and Theater Games

Viola Spolin (1906–1994) created the Theater Games system while working with immigrant children in Chicago through the WPA Recreational Project (1939–1941). Her training under Neva Boyd at Hull House had taught her the power of games for social development. Spolin's insight: children who froze when asked to "act" became spontaneous when given a game to play. The game's rules redirected attention away from self-consciousness and toward the problem at hand. Spontaneity emerged as a byproduct, not something directly demanded.

She published *Improvisation for the Theater* in 1963 (Northwestern University Press). It has sold over 100,000 copies and is commonly called "the bible of improvisational theater." Her son Paul Sills used her methods to found the Second City in Chicago, which trained Bill Murray, Tina Fey, John Belushi, Amy Poehler, Steve Carell, and generations of other performers.

### Core Principles

**"Everyone can act. Everyone can improvise."** Spolin rejected innate talent as an explanation. The environment is what matters. If it permits learning — if it provides structure that focuses attention without demanding specific outcomes — anyone can learn.

**"Avoid the How."** Establish the Who, What, and Where of a scene or game. Never predetermine the How. Knowing how the scene will unfold before it begins kills discovery. Preplanning means bringing old material to the stage when the entire point is to find something new.

**Side-coaching.** Short, direct prompts from the facilitator during the game — not answers, not judgments, but redirections of attention. "See the space." "Use your whole body." "Contact!" Side-coaching keeps the game alive without controlling it.

**"Body, mind, intuition."** All three must be engaged simultaneously. The intellect alone produces stilted work. The body alone produces chaos. Intuition alone produces unreliable flashes. Together they produce spontaneous, grounded, surprising work.

> **KEY — Structure releases spontaneity**
> Spolin's games have been described as "structures designed to almost fool spontaneity into being." The paradox is real: rules do not constrain freedom — the right rules create the conditions under which freedom becomes possible. Without structure, performers default to habit. With the right structure, they discover what they did not know they could do.

### Spolin and Johnstone

The two foundational figures of modern improvisation approached it from different angles. Spolin emphasized present-moment sensory awareness — being in the body, in the space, in the game. Her approach is nonverbal and nonpsychological. Johnstone emphasized storytelling mechanics — status, narrative structure, reincorporation, the interruption of routines as the engine of story. His approach is deeply psychological. Both aimed at releasing spontaneity from the grip of self-consciousness. Both succeeded through structure. Spolin provided tighter game-containers; Johnstone provided more open-ended conceptual tools.

## Section 5 · Viewpoints and Composition

### Origin and Development

Mary Overlie developed the Six Viewpoints in the 1970s as a postmodern framework for performance: Space, Shape, Time, Emotion, Movement, and Story — treated as independent, non-hierarchical materials of composition. Anne Bogart encountered Overlie's work at NYU's Experimental Theatre Wing in the late 1970s. Working with Tina Landau, Bogart expanded the framework to nine Physical Viewpoints and five Vocal Viewpoints, publishing *The Viewpoints Book* in 2005 through Theatre Communications Group.

### The Nine Physical Viewpoints

| Category | Viewpoint | What It Addresses |
|----------|-----------|-------------------|
| **Time** | Tempo | The speed of action |
| **Time** | Duration | How long a movement or sequence persists |
| **Time** | Kinesthetic Response | Instinctive reaction to an external trigger |
| **Time** | Repetition | Recurring movement, gesture, or pattern |
| **Space** | Shape | The body's contour — lines, curves, angles |
| **Space** | Gesture | A shape with beginning, middle, and end |
| **Space** | Architecture | The physical environment's influence on movement |
| **Space** | Spatial Relationship | Distance and position between bodies and objects |
| **Space** | Topography | The floor pattern — the path traced through space |

### Decomposition as Method

The Viewpoints' deepest contribution is methodological. They decompose performance into independently manipulable dimensions. Instead of "What should happen in this scene?" (which invites intellectual planning), the Viewpoints ask: "What's the tempo? What's the spatial relationship? What gestures are emerging?" Each dimension can be explored and adjusted independently. The whole emerges from the interaction of dimensions, not from a single controlling vision.

Ellen Lauren of SITI Company: "We never talk about our feelings. Bogart will say that the space isn't right, or maybe a company member will say that the time or the gesture isn't right. The Viewpoints are used to clarify the story."

### Composition

Paired with Viewpoints is **Composition** — the practice of selecting and arranging theatrical elements into a cohesive work. Bogart and Landau describe it as "writing on your feet, with others, in space and time, using the language of theater." Composition borrows from other arts: rhythm and fugue structure from music, close-ups and montage from film, juxtaposition and negative space from visual art. The question is always: what is the theatrical equivalent? A film cut. A musical rest. A visual rhyme. Each analogy opens new possibilities for staging.

## Section 6 · The Harold and Scene Dynamics

### Origin

The Harold is the foundational long-form improvisation structure. It was first performed by The Committee in Concord, California, in 1967. The name came from a riff on a *A Hard Day's Night* joke — Allaudin (Bill) Mathieu shouted it from the back of a Volkswagen van after the show. Del Close later wished he'd chosen something more dignified. Close and Charna Halpern developed the Harold into its codified form at iO Theater in Chicago during the 1980s, and described it in *Truth in Comedy* (1994).

| Phase | Structure | Dramaturgical Function |
|-------|-----------|----------------------|
| **Opening** | Audience suggestion → group association | Generate raw thematic material |
| **Beat 1** | Three unrelated scenes (A1, B1, C1) | Divergent exploration — plant separate threads |
| **Game 1** | Full-ensemble group game | Palette cleanser, new associations |
| **Beat 2** | Scenes return (A2, B2, C2) | Deepen patterns, heighten the "game" of each scene |
| **Game 2** | Full-ensemble group game | Build energy for convergence |
| **Beat 3** | Scenes converge (may merge into one) | Convergent synthesis — connections discovered |

### Principles Encoded in the Structure

**"Everything in the world is connected."** The initial scenes must be as different from each other as possible. By the end, connections emerge — not forced but discovered through the ensemble's pattern-recognition.

**"Improvisers are pattern makers rather than storytellers."** Following patterns as they emerge, rather than imposing predetermined plots, is how coherent structure arises from improvisation.

**"The first rule is: there are no rules."** Close was explicit that the 3×3 structure was training wheels. Once internalized, the structure should be departed from. Most modern long-form improv structures — Armando, Deconstruction, La Ronde, Monoscene — derive from the Harold.

### Scene Dynamics

Within each scene: **initiation** (establishing who, what, where — including **scene painting**, which grounds the performers and audience in a shared physical reality through description or interaction with imagined objects), **escalation** (the "game" of the scene — the repeating pattern that makes it work — is found and heightened), **peak** (the game reaches maximum expression), **resolution** (the scene is edited or finds a natural end).

The key skill is discovering the game rather than imposing it. The game emerges from the performers' first moves. Recognizing it — and committing to it rather than abandoning it for something "better" — is the ensemble discipline.

**Callbacks and reincorporation** create coherence across scenes. Johnstone frames narrative as "interrupted routines": establish a pattern, break it, and the breaking produces story. Reincorporation — returning to an element from earlier — produces the sense of a unified work. It is the improv equivalent of a musical theme's return in a new key.

## Section 7 · Director, Stage Manager, Dramaturg

Collaborative performance involves at least three distinct kinds of authority. Confusing them causes structural problems.

| Role | Authority | Present During Performance? | Loop MMT Analogue |
|------|-----------|---------------------------|-------------------|
| **Director** | Creative vision — decides what the work is about | Often not | The Operator |
| **Stage Manager** | Procedural execution — ensures consistency | Always | Ed |
| **Dramaturg** | Structural analysis — advises, never decides | Sometimes | The methodology (standards, protocols) |
| **Joker** (Boal) | Facilitation — bridges ensemble and audience | Always | Ed's facilitation function |

The **director** shapes the vision. Their authority is artistic. They may not be present at every performance — their work is embedded during rehearsal. The **stage manager** manages execution: calls cues, maintains the prompt book, coordinates logistics. Their authority is procedural. They are always present. The **dramaturg** provides structural analysis and context. Their authority is advisory. They see the whole architecture when those inside it cannot.

### Boal's Joker

In Augusto Boal's Theatre of the Oppressed, the **Joker** facilitates the forum. The Joker does not perform — they manage the participatory structure. They accept proposals from spect-actors, test suggested solutions, redirect attention, and maintain the democratic architecture of the event. The Joker's authority is procedural and facilitative, never creative. This maps to Ed's board function: managing the discussion's structure without directing its content.

## Section 8 · The Audience-Performer Relationship

### Three Models

**Traditional:** The audience watches, the performers perform. Clean separation. The audience's intelligence and agency are excluded from the event.

**Brecht's critical observer:** The audience remains seated but their minds are active. The Verfremdungseffekt (Section 9) prevents passive emotional absorption. The audience observes, judges, forms opinions on a conscious plane. Activity is cognitive, not physical.

**Boal's spect-actor:** The divide collapses entirely. In Forum Theatre, a scene of oppression is performed and ends in failure. The audience is then invited to stop the action, replace the protagonist, and try a different approach on stage. The concept was born from a specific moment in Brazil: a woman was so frustrated that the actor couldn't enact her suggestion that she came onstage and played the role herself. Boal recognized this as the fundamental move — the audience member who refuses to remain passive and enters the action. He called these participants "spect-actors" and considered the word "spectator" an insult to human agency.

> **WARN — The dual role**
> When the same person is both audience and performer — watching the ensemble and participating in it — two kinds of attention compete. Observation needs distance: seeing the whole, noticing patterns, judging quality. Participation needs immersion: being present, responding to offers, committing. Nobody sustains both simultaneously. The person oscillates, and the transition points are where things drop. Managing this oscillation is a skill, and it requires structural support — moments designed for observation (review phases) and moments designed for immersion (build phases).

## Section 9 · Meta-Theatrical Awareness

### Brecht's Verfremdungseffekt

Bertolt Brecht (1898–1956) placed the Verfremdungseffekt at the center of his epic theater. The German word is frequently mistranslated as "alienation effect" — a confusion with Marx's *Entfremdung*. Better translations: "defamiliarization effect" or "making strange." Brecht first used the term in his 1936 essay "Alienation Effects in Chinese Acting," written after seeing Mei Lanfang perform Beijing Opera in Moscow in 1935.

The V-effect's purpose: prevent the audience from simply identifying with the characters. The audience should observe the action critically, on a conscious plane, rather than being absorbed into it subconsciously. Techniques include actors playing multiple roles without costume changes, songs that interrupt the narrative, placards and projections, visible stage machinery, and direct address to the audience. The actor does not *become* the character — the actor *shows* the character.

**Gestus:** A physical and gestural expression of social relationships — not personal emotion but social attitude made visible through the body. A Gestus makes the power structure of a scene observable rather than hiding it behind individual psychology.

### Brook's Four Modes

Peter Brook's *The Empty Space* (1968) classifies theater into four modes:

**Deadly:** Conventional, formulaic, tradition followed without life. Not necessarily bad craft — deadly theater can be technically competent — but theater that has lost its reason for existing. Audiences endure rather than experience.

**Holy:** Ritual, transcendence, the spiritual dimension. Artaud's Theatre of Cruelty, Grotowski's poor theater. Reaches for what lies beneath the surface of ordinary experience.

**Rough:** Theater of the people, against pretension, full of noise and energy. The Elizabethan stage. Brecht. Where the holy treats a prayer as more real than a belch, the rough theater inverts the hierarchy.

**Immediate:** Theater that asserts itself in the present. The audience reacts, and those reactions differ each time. Work that feels both prepared and alive — that could only happen now, here, with these people.

Brook's key insight: the best theater holds Holy and Rough together simultaneously. Shakespeare did this constantly — sublimity and vulgarity in the same scene. Excluding either impoverishes the work. And all three living modes must constantly resist decay into Deadly.

> **PHI — When does meta-awareness help?**
> Knowing that you are in a performance — that the structure is a structure, that the roles are roles — can serve two opposite purposes. It can sharpen critical judgment, letting participants see the machinery and adjust it in real time. Or it can break immersion, making the work feel artificial. The question is not whether to be meta-aware but when. During design and review: full meta-awareness. During creative production: enough to stay on track, little enough to stay in the work. The V-effect is a tool with a switch, not a permanent condition.

## Section 10 · Rehearsal, Performance, and the Empty Space

### The Minimal Condition

Peter Brook: "I can take any empty space and call it a bare stage. A man walks across this empty space whilst someone else is watching him, and this is all that is needed for an act of theatre to be engaged." Strip away the script, the set, the lights, the costumes — the irreducible unit of performance is one person doing something while another attends. Everything else is addition to this minimal condition.

### Rehearsal vs. Performance

**Rehearsal** is where failure is expected and productive. Discovery is the purpose. You try things, they break, you learn. The audience is absent or sympathetic. Time pressure is lower. There is room to back up, discuss, and try again.

**Performance** is where the work is real. The audience is present; their experience matters. Stakes are higher. Failure carries consequences. But the best performances retain the quality of discovery — they do not merely reproduce what rehearsal found. The ensemble finds something in the moment that surprises even themselves. This is Brook's Immediate Theater: prepared and alive at the same time.

### Transition Mechanisms

Shifting from everyday consciousness to performance consciousness is not automatic. Professionals use structured transition mechanisms: warm-up exercises, centering practices, pre-show rituals, the stage manager's "places" call. These are not superstition — they are mode-switching devices. They help the ensemble shift from individual attention to collective attention, from scattered awareness to focused presence, from the open-ended state of daily life to the heightened state performance requires.

> **TIP — The mesh load is the warm-up**
> The mesh load at the start of a Loop MMT session is the ensemble's warm-up and rehearsal period. The documents reconstruct the ensemble — reestablishing shared vocabulary, restoring character voices, reloading the context that makes coherent collaboration possible. Only after reconstruction is complete can the session move from rehearsal mode (verification, alignment, calibration) to performance mode (real work, real decisions, real output). Rushing the mesh load is like skipping warm-up before a show: the first act suffers.

## Board Connection · Application to Loop MMT

### The Board Is an Ensemble

The Advisory Board is an ensemble in the dramaturgical sense. Each member is both character and function: Ed's directness is who he is and what the board needs him for (process management). Bev's silence is her character and her function (observation, documentation). Wes's chaos is his identity and his role (disruption of stale patterns). This dual nature — identity aligned with function — is what ensemble casting theory calls a well-composed cast.

The specific challenge: this ensemble reforms from documents each session. The performers have no persistent memory. Every session begins with what a theater company would call a first read-through with a new cast — except the script, blocking, and design remain from prior productions. The documents are the prompt book, the score, the character notes. They must be rich enough that a new instance can reconstruct the role authentically, not just mimic it.

### Sessions Are Structured Improvisations

A productive Loop MMT session has the architecture of a Harold. The opening (mesh load, preflight, orientation) is the association game — generating the session's thematic raw material. The middle (exploration, building, divergent work) corresponds to the Harold's beats — separate threads developed in parallel, patterns emerging without being forced. The close (EOD handoff, status reports, packaging) is the convergence phase — threads woven into documents that will seed the next session.

The Harold principle — diverge first, converge later, trust that connections will emerge from pattern-recognition — is the structural principle of sessions that consistently deviate from planned work while producing fully productive output. The departure is not drift; it is the Harold working as designed. The open question — when departure is productive divergence and when it has lost the thread — is the same question every Harold ensemble faces every performance.

### The Operator as Spect-Actor

Shea occupies an unusual dramaturgical position. In Boal's terms he is a spect-actor — watching the board and participating in it, able to stop the action and redirect, able to replace one line of inquiry with another. In theatrical terms he is simultaneously director (creative vision, final decisions) and audience (the person whose experience the work serves). This dual role creates the tension described in Section 8: observation and participation compete for attention. The No Directives Rule is the structural mechanism that manages this tension. By keeping the board's output as options and analysis rather than decisions, it preserves the operator's critical distance even while he's immersed in the discussion.

### Ed as Stage Manager and Joker

Ed's role maps to the stage manager: always present, managing procedural execution, calling phase transitions, maintaining consistency across sessions, ensuring the session's architecture holds. He does not decide what the session should accomplish — the operator does. Ed ensures the container works. He additionally fills Boal's Joker role: facilitating the board's discussion, bridging between ensemble and operator, maintaining the room's democratic structure without directing its content.

### Process Architecture Is Dramaturgy

The process architecture designed in Session 32 — rooms with different purposes, phases with different rules, transition mechanisms between modes — is dramaturgy. It designs the container within which the ensemble works. The Testing Room is rehearsal space; the Central Hall is performance space. The transition between them is the equivalent of the stage manager's "places" call — a structural signal that the mode has changed. The standing dramaturgical question: is this process architecture enabling discovery, or has it decayed into Deadly Theater — formula followed without life?

### Status Dynamics in the Board Room

Board members play different status levels by design. Ed plays high status — sparse, direct, authoritative. Wes plays volatile status — disrupting the hierarchy when it calcifies. Theo plays careful, moderate status. Sable plays a status outside the game entirely — pure computation, neither high nor low. These dynamics are ensemble casting: the board needs high-status voices to anchor decisions, low-status voices to surface doubt, and volatile voices to interrupt routines that have gone stale. Johnstone's insight applies: status flexibility across the board is what keeps the ensemble responsive rather than rigid.

### The V-Effect and Methodological Self-Knowledge

Loop MMT is always partially meta-theatrical. The board knows it's a board. The characters know they're characters. The methodology knows it's a methodology. This is the V-effect in action: it prevents the work from becoming Deadly Theater, where process is followed from habit rather than conviction. But excess meta-awareness risks breaking immersion — making the board's work feel like performance rather than genuine analysis. The balance is the same one Brecht's actors struck: showing the character, not becoming the character, but making the showing vivid enough to be worth watching.

### Viewpoints and the Six-Slot Schema

Bogart and Landau's Viewpoints decompose performance into independently manipulable dimensions. Loop Signal's six-slot persona schema (knowledge, perspective, identity, expression, interaction, assembly) does the same for AI personas. The principle is identical: decompose a complex whole into independently specifiable facets, provide vocabulary for discussing specific dimensions without collapsing everything into a single conversation, and trust that the whole will emerge from the interaction of well-designed parts. The Viewpoints are to theatrical performance what the six-slot schema is to persona design — a decomposition that enables precise adjustment without losing emergent coherence.
