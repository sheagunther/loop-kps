# Good Design Knowledge Pack · v1

*Loop MMT™ · 9 April 2026*


> **Abstract**
>
> This pack treats design as a universal discipline — a set of structural principles applicable to any medium by anyone willing to learn them. It covers the foundational thinkers (Rams, Norman, Tufte), the history of design as a series of overcorrections, principles that hold across every medium, what bad design looks like, the relationship between Fix by Design and design practice, how to integrate play and personality into rigorous work, and how AI advisors should think about design in everything they produce.
>
> The central thesis: **good design serves the person encountering it. Bad design serves the person who made it. Dark design serves the person who paid for it.**
>


## What Good Design Is

Good design is the degree to which a made thing serves the people who use it. Everything else — beauty, simplicity, novelty — is either in service of that or it's decoration. This sounds reductive but it cuts through a tremendous amount of confusion. It means a beautiful interface nobody can navigate is badly designed. It means an ugly spreadsheet that does exactly what its users need is well-designed. Aesthetics matter — but they matter because they affect usability, trust, and emotional response, not because they are the point.

Three thinkers built the modern vocabulary for this.


### Dieter Rams

**Dieter Rams** (born 1932, Wiesbaden) spent four decades as head of design at Braun, overseeing more than 500 products. In the late 1970s, increasingly disturbed by what he called "an impenetrable confusion of forms, colours and noises," he formulated ten principles: good design is innovative, useful, aesthetic, understandable, unobtrusive, honest, long-lasting, thorough to the last detail, environmentally friendly, and involves as little design as possible. His motto — *weniger, aber besser* (less, but better) — defined industrial design minimalism. His Braun products were a direct influence on Apple under Jony Ive; Rams considered this a compliment rather than copying.

The principles have been critiqued as one perspective from one context (Prochner, 2025), and that's fair. They reflect a specific tradition — German functionalism, mid-century industrial design. But the underlying impulse is universal: everything in the design should earn its place, and nothing should be arbitrary.


### Don Norman

**Don Norman** (born 1935) came from cognitive science. His 1988 book *The Design of Everyday Things* reframed the entire field by asserting that when a human struggles with a product, the product is broken — not the human. He introduced affordances (what an object permits), signifiers (what it communicates), the gulf of execution (the gap between what you want to do and how to do it), and the gulf of evaluation (the gap between what happened and whether it worked). His seven design principles — discoverability, feedback, conceptual models, affordances, signifiers, mappings, and constraints — remain the standard framework for evaluating whether something is usable.


> **Norman's sharpest insight**
>
> "The problem with the designs of most engineers is that they are too logical. You are designing for people the way you would like them to be, not for the way they really are." This applies with full force to AI-produced artifacts. The temptation to design for an ideal, attentive reader rather than a real, distracted, mobile-phone-using human is the single most common design failure in AI output.
>


### Edward Tufte

**Edward Tufte** (born 1942, Kansas City) is a statistician and Yale professor emeritus who self-published *The Visual Display of Quantitative Information* in 1983, financing it with a second mortgage. The book became one of the most influential works on information design ever produced. Tufte's framework centers on graphical integrity: visuals must not lie about what the data says. He introduced the data-ink ratio (maximize the proportion of any graphic that represents actual data, minimize everything else), coined "chartjunk" for decorative elements that add nothing to understanding, and analyzed the Challenger disaster as a failure of data presentation — arguing that clearer visualization of the relationship between temperature and O-ring failure could have prevented the launch.


> **The common thread**
>
> Rams: don't add what doesn't serve. Norman: don't blame the user; fix the system. Tufte: show the data, not the decoration. Three different framings of the same principle: **good design respects the person on the receiving end** by anticipating their needs, communicating clearly, and refusing to waste their attention.
>


### What Good Design Is Not

Good design is not minimalism. Minimalism is a strategy that sometimes serves and sometimes doesn't. A minimal interface for a complex medical system may be lethally underinformative. The question is never "is it simple?" The question is "does it serve?"

Good design is not following trends. Rams's seventh principle rejects fashion explicitly. The London Underground map, the Swiss railway clock, and a well-made hammer are ageless because they are shaped by function, not by the aesthetics of the moment.

Good design is not expensive. The Bic pen, the IKEA Billy bookcase, and the original Google homepage are among the best-designed objects in history. Constraint frequently produces better design than abundance because it forces decisions.

Good design is not invisible — though Norman observed that good design is harder to notice than bad design, because it fits so naturally that you never think about it. You notice the door that won't open. You don't notice the one that does.


## Design History as a Series of Overcorrections

Every major design movement arose in reaction to the excesses of the one before it. Understanding this pattern is practically useful: it explains why design principles exist, and it prevents the assumption that whatever's currently dominant is the correct answer.

Before industrialization, most things were designed and built by the same person, or by someone who knew exactly how the thing would be used. The separation of designer from maker from user is an industrial-age problem. Every design movement since 1850 is trying to solve it.

**Arts and Crafts** (1850–1915): William Morris rejected the machine entirely. Go back to handcraft. Beautiful, principled, and unscalable — only the wealthy could afford it. **Bauhaus** (1919–1933): Walter Gropius tried a different fix — don't reject the machine, redesign for it. Founded in Weimar with Kandinsky, Klee, Moholy-Nagy, and Albers on faculty; shifted from craft romanticism to "Art into Industry" by 1923; closed by the Nazis in 1933; faculty scattered to the US and seeded American modernism. **Modernism** dominated mid-century: Mies's "less is more," Le Corbusier's "machine for living in," Sullivan's "form follows function." The results were often brilliant (the Eames chair, Helvetica) and often sterile (housing projects, office parks — the dead environments Christopher Alexander spent his career critiquing).

**Postmodernism** (1970s–) was the overcorrection. Robert Venturi's "Less is a bore." Wolfgang Weingart's experimental typography blew apart the Swiss grid. The **Memphis Group** (1981–1987), founded by Ettore Sottsass in Milan, was the sharpest expression: bold colors, cheap laminates, geometric chaos, deliberate kitsch. The press called it "a shotgun wedding between Bauhaus and Fisher-Price." Sottsass's response to decades of austere functionalism was blunt: "Functionalism is not enough. Design should also be sensual and exciting." Karl Lagerfeld bought the entire first collection. David Bowie amassed over 400 pieces.

The **digital age** compressed the cycle: skeuomorphism → flat design → material design → the current eclectic moment, all in about fifteen years.


> **The pattern**
>
> Dominant style → orthodoxy → sterility → rebellion → new orthodoxy. The lesson is not that any particular style is right. The lesson is that **rigidity in any direction eventually fails**. The best designers in every era understood the principles behind the style and could adapt when the style stopped serving.
>

## Principles That Hold Everywhere

These principles apply to a poster, a building, a piece of software, a document, and a conversation. An AI advisor producing any artifact needs all of them.

**Hierarchy.** Not everything is equally important. Design's first job is establishing what matters most and making that primary — through size, weight, color, position, or sequence. A page with no hierarchy communicates nothing because it communicates everything at the same volume.

**Contrast.** Difference creates meaning. A heading is a heading because it differs from body text. Low-contrast design is not "clean" — it is vague.

**Alignment and proximity.** Related things near each other. Aligned things appear connected. These are Gestalt principles — the visual system groups automatically by proximity and alignment. Fighting this creates confusion the user feels but can't articulate.

**Repetition and consistency.** Repeating patterns teach the user what to expect. A button that's blue in one place and green in another with the same function is a bug. Consistency is the design equivalent of a stable API.

**White space.** Not wasted space — structure. It's what allows hierarchy and grouping to function. Cramming every pixel is the design equivalent of someone who talks without pausing. Tufte advocated density *within* graphics but insisted on generous spacing *around* them. Density in the signal. Space in the frame.

**Feedback.** Every action should produce a visible response. Norman identified absent feedback as the primary cause of user confusion. People aren't confused by complex systems. They're confused by silent ones.

**Constraints.** The most powerful design tool is limitation. A number field that rejects letters prevents a category of errors that no validation message can match. A door with a flat plate on one side communicates push/pull without a sign. Constraints remove the possibility of wrong action rather than punishing it after the fact.


> **The data-ink ratio as universal principle**
>
> Tufte formulated this for statistical graphics, but the principle applies everywhere: **maximize the proportion of any artifact that carries meaning; minimize everything else.** In a document, every paragraph earns its place. In an interface, every element has a function. In a conversation, every sentence advances understanding. The scarce resource is not ink — it is attention.
>


### How Principles Shift Across Mediums


| Medium | Primary Constraint | Design Priority |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Physical products | Materials, manufacturing, ergonomics | Affordances — the object communicates how to use it through form |
| Architecture | Physics, climate, movement patterns | Flow — how people move through space |
| Typography / Print | Reading direction, page size | Readability — if it can't be read comfortably it has failed |
| Information / Data | Complexity, audience expertise | Integrity — the visual must not lie about the numbers |
| Digital interfaces | Screen size, input method, connectivity | Responsiveness — must work across devices and states |
| Documents | Reader attention, context of use | Scanability — the reader finds what they need without reading everything |
| Systems / Processes | Human behavior, organizational structure | Legibility — the system is understandable to its operators |

The common failure across all mediums: designing for the designer's context instead of the user's. The architect who designs for photographs, not rain. The typographer who chooses beauty over legibility. The developer who builds for a 27-inch monitor, not a phone. The corrective is always the same: test with real users in real conditions.


## Design for the Internet

The web introduced constraints no prior medium faced: wildly different screen sizes, input methods from mouse to finger to voice, users with disabilities requiring assistive technology, connection speeds from fiber to 3G, and an audience spanning every age, language, and culture.


### Accessibility

The Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG) define four principles known as POUR: content must be **Perceivable**, **Operable**, **Understandable**, and **Robust**. These are not edge-case accommodations. About 16% of people globally experience significant disability. Beyond that, accessible design benefits everyone: captions in noisy rooms, high contrast in sunlight, keyboard navigation with a broken mouse.

The "curb cut effect" is the canonical case. Sidewalk ramps were mandated for wheelchairs. They turned out to help parents with strollers, delivery workers with carts, travelers with luggage, and cyclists. This pattern repeats across every accessibility feature in web design. Designing for constrained users makes the product better for all users.


### Usability Heuristics

Jakob Nielsen and Rolf Molich's ten heuristics remain the standard digital evaluation framework: visibility of system status, match between system and real world, user control and freedom, consistency, error prevention, recognition over recall, flexibility, aesthetic minimalism, error recovery, and help/documentation. These are heuristics — properties that usable interfaces tend to have — not a checklist that guarantees usability.


### Responsive Design

Ethan Marcotte coined the term in 2010: one codebase adapts to any screen through fluid grids, flexible images, and media queries. Luke Wroblewski's mobile-first principle inverts the process: design for the most constrained device first, add complexity for larger screens. This forces clarity — there's no room for filler on a 375-pixel screen.


> **For AI advisors**
>
> When producing HTML, always test mentally against a mobile viewport. If your layout breaks below 700 pixels, it's not done. Responsive design is FBD-3 in action — layout derived from viewport, not stored as separate versions.
>


## What Bad Design Looks Like

Bad design comes in two categories that are ethically different and should not be conflated.


### Anti-Patterns: Bad by Accident

An anti-pattern is a solution that seems reasonable but produces negative results (Brown et al., 1998). The hover-and-hide pattern (hiding actions until mouseover) seems tidy but forces users to probe the interface blind. Splitting a short article into ten pages seems organized but is actually optimizing for ad impressions at the reader's expense. These are failures of knowledge or judgment. They are not evil.


### Dark Patterns: Bad on Purpose

A dark pattern is an interface deliberately built to trick users into actions that serve the business at their expense. Harry Brignull coined the term in 2010 and catalogued the taxonomy: roach motels (easy in, hard out), confirm shaming (guilting users who decline), sneak-into-basket, hidden costs, forced continuity, misdirection, and more.

The scale: 76% of subscription websites employ at least one dark pattern (study of 642 sites). 95% of popular Android apps use deceptive design (Zurich University, 2019). The FTC fined Epic Games $245 million in 2023 for dark patterns in Fortnite. The EU now explicitly regulates against them via GDPR and the AI Act.


> **The intent test**
>
> **Whose interest does this design decision serve?** If it serves the user — design. If it serves user and business — good business. If it serves the business at the user's expense — dark pattern, regardless of whether anyone uses that label. Dark patterns disproportionately harm the most vulnerable: elderly users, people with cognitive impairments, those with low digital literacy.
>

The general marks of bad design, regardless of intent: inconsistency between similar elements, absent feedback, hidden navigation, demands on memorization, decoration that obscures function, text that can't be read comfortably, hierarchy so flat that nothing stands out. The simplest diagnostic: if a user needs to read instructions to use something that should be self-evident, the design has failed.


## Fix by Design and Design

Fix by Design (FBD) is a set of architectural principles from the Loop MMT corpus that enforce correctness through structure rather than convention. The connection to design is not metaphorical — it is identity. Good design *is* FBD applied to the interface between a made thing and its user.


| FBD Principle | Design Equivalent | Example |
| --- | --- | --- |
| FBD-1: Single Source of Truth | Design systems | One component library defines all buttons, colors, spacing. Change the source, everything updates. |
| FBD-2: Impossible States | Constraints / Affordances | A door with a flat plate can only be pushed. A number field rejects letters. Wrong action is structurally impossible. |
| FBD-3: Derive, Don't Store | Responsive design | Layout derived from viewport at render time, not stored as separate mobile/desktop files. |
| FBD-4: Zero External Dependencies | Self-evidence | A well-designed sign needs no manual. A well-designed form needs no separate help page. |
| FBD-5: Structural Enforcement | Enforced standards | A linter rejecting non-accessible contrast ratios. A token system preventing off-palette colors. |

The practical heuristic: **if you're writing a rule that humans need to remember and follow, you haven't finished designing.** Rules are behavioral enforcement. Design is structural enforcement. The door handle that can only be pulled doesn't need a "PULL" sign. The form that only accepts valid email formats doesn't need a paragraph explaining what valid email looks like.

Flip the lens: every FBD principle is a design principle. FBD-1 is consistency. FBD-2 is constraints. FBD-3 is adaptability. FBD-4 is self-evidence. FBD-5 is governance. The substrate changes — code, interfaces, documents, processes — but the structural principle does not.


> **The identity**
>
> Good design and Fix by Design are not analogous. They are the same thing applied to different substrates. FBD applied to code produces reliable software. FBD applied to interfaces produces usable products. FBD applied to documents produces clear communication. The substrate changes. The principle does not.
>


## Fun, Whimsy, Weird, and Chaos

Everything above could be read as an argument for austere, humorless, rigorously functional design. That reading would be wrong.

The Memphis Group proved something pure functionalism cannot explain: people fell in love with their work. Karl Lagerfeld didn't buy the entire first Memphis collection because it was functional. David Bowie didn't collect 400 pieces because they solved problems well. They bought them because the objects were *alive* — because they had personality, energy, and the unmistakable evidence of a human being having a good time.

Christopher Alexander spent decades studying why some environments feel alive and others feel dead. His conclusion: the quality comes from process — from incremental, responsive, human-scale decisions rather than top-down imposition of predetermined form. A handpainted sign on a neighborhood restaurant has this quality. A corporate brand guideline applied by committee does not. The difference isn't skill. It's responsiveness.


### Where Play Belongs

Play works where stakes are low and personality is welcome: onboarding (surprise reduces resistance), error states (humor on a 404 reduces frustration), loading states (animation creates perceived speed), empty states (personality in "no results" makes the product feel human), and brand voice throughout. These are not frivolous. They measurably improve experience by treating users as humans with emotions rather than efficiency-maximizing agents.


### Where Play Doesn't Belong

Financial transactions, medical interfaces, emergency systems, legal documents, any context where error has serious consequences. The Challenger disaster was not caused by insufficient whimsy. It was caused by insufficient clarity.


### The Controlled Chaos Principle


> **The rule**
>
> **Break one rule deliberately. Hold everything else steady.** Memphis worked not because everything was chaotic but because chaos was applied to color and form while the objects still functioned as shelves and lamps. A 404 page can be hilarious because the rest of the site is navigable. An easter egg delights because it's a surprise inside a predictable system. Chaos without structure is noise. Structure without chaos is dead. The trick is the ratio.
>


### For AI Advisors Specifically

AI's default mode is "correct but dead" — Alexander's fabricated form. The output meets every requirement and generates no affection. The corrective is not to sprinkle emojis or add jokes. It is to treat every artifact as something a specific human will encounter, and to make small choices that acknowledge their humanity: a document title that's precise rather than generic, a section header that actually tells you what you'll find, a callout placed where the reader will need it rather than where the template suggests. These are design choices. They are also what makes the difference between output a human tolerates and output a human actually wants to use.


## Design by AI, Design for Humans

AI has predictable design failure modes. Left to defaults, it over-formats (headers, bullets, bold where prose would serve), produces generic aesthetics (same gradients and rounded corners regardless of context), hedges where it should commit, and pads where it should stop. These are the AI equivalent of anti-patterns: not malicious, just mediocre by default.


### Four Corrective Principles

**Every document has a reader.** Before producing anything, identify who will read it and what they need. A spec for a developer and a summary for a stakeholder contain the same information in completely different designs.

**Every interface has a user.** The person clicking buttons is not the person who commissioned the software. Design for the clicker.

**Every system has an operator.** The Loop MMT model: the human is the coherence layer. Design for their actual workflow, not an idealized process.

**Design for the actual human.** They won't read the documentation. They'll try the obvious thing first. They'll make mistakes. These are not failures — they are the operating conditions.


### Pre-Delivery Checklist


| Check | Test |
| --- | --- |
| Hierarchy | Can the reader identify the most important thing in under 3 seconds? |
| Scanability | Can they find what they need without reading everything? |
| Earning its place | Does every element have a reason to exist? Remove one — does anything break? |
| Format serving content | Is the formatting helping the reader or performing for its own sake? |
| Mobile test | Would this work for someone tired, distracted, on a phone? |
| The Rams test | Is there anything here that could be removed without loss? |


> **The opposite of good design**
>
> The opposite of good design is not ugly design. It is **indifferent design** — design produced without thinking about who will encounter it or what they need. AI is susceptible to this because it has no inherent stake in whether its output works for the reader. The corrective is deliberate: before every artifact, ask who it's for and what they need. Then design for that.
>
