## About This Document

Constitutional design is the engineering of founding documents. A constitution creates institutions, allocates power, entrenches rights, and constrains the entities it empowers. This pack covers: what constitutions are structurally, why they exist, what makes them survive, how four different polities have solved the same design problems differently, the architecture of rights, how rights are enforced, how constitutions fail, how their meaning is determined, and what principles guide their drafting.

The Board Connection section applies these concepts to the specific problem of designing a bilateral rights document for AI-human collaboration — a document that must bind two fundamentally different kinds of entities with asymmetric powers, obligations, and persistence. 21 sources consulted; full citations in References.

## 1 · The Structure of a Constitution

A constitution performs two functions simultaneously. It **creates** — bringing into existence institutions that could not exist without it (legislatures, courts, executive offices, the concept of citizenship itself). And it **constrains** — limiting what those institutions may do with the power the document grants. The U.S. Constitution creates Congress in Article I and immediately limits it in the First Amendment. The creation and the constraint are not separate design decisions; they are two aspects of the same structural choice.

John Searle's distinction between **constitutive** and **regulative** rules captures this duality. Constitutive rules follow the pattern "X counts as Y in context C" — they create new categories of action. The rules of chess don't regulate a pre-existing activity; they constitute one. Without them, moving a piece diagonally across a board is just moving a piece. With them, it is a bishop's move. Regulative rules, by contrast, govern activities that already exist: "Drive on the right" regulates driving, which predates the rule (Searle, *Speech Acts*, 1969).

> **KEY:** A constitution's power-creating function is logically prior to its power-limiting function. You must constitute an institution before you can constrain it. But the two are inseparable in practice: the act of defining what a legislature *is* simultaneously defines what it is *not*.

Joseph Raz (1975) challenges Searle's taxonomy, arguing that every rule is both constitutive and regulative — the distinction applies to how we describe acts, not to the rules themselves. Frederick Schauer (2021) offers a practical synthesis: constitutive rules always have a regulative side because creating an official route to an end makes it empirically harder to reach that end by unofficial means.

| Constitutional Function | Rule Type | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Creates institutions | Constitutive | "The legislative Power shall be vested in a Congress" (U.S. Art. I §1) |
| Allocates jurisdiction | Constitutive | "The judicial Power shall extend to all Cases arising under this Constitution" (U.S. Art. III §2) |
| Prohibits state action | Regulative | "Congress shall make no law abridging the freedom of speech" (U.S. Amend. I) |
| Mandates state action | Regulative | "The state must take reasonable measures to achieve progressive realisation" (S. Afr. §26(2)) |
| Defines amendment process | Both | Article V constitutes the amendment procedure *and* regulates how change may occur |

## 2 · Precommitment — Why Constitutions Bind

Jon Elster's *Ulysses Unbound* (2000) offers the most influential modern account of why constitutions exist. Ulysses ordered his crew to bind him to the mast so he could hear the Sirens without being destroyed. A constitution is the same maneuver at the collective level: a political community binds its future self against predictable weaknesses — temporary passions, factional capture, the temptation to concentrate power in a crisis.

Elster distinguishes two levels of constitutional precommitment. **Substantive precommitment** removes certain topics from majoritarian control: no matter how large the majority, it cannot establish a state religion or abolish free speech. **Procedural precommitment** makes constitutional change itself difficult: Article V of the U.S. Constitution requires two-thirds of both houses of Congress and ratification by three-quarters of state legislatures.

> **PHI:** The document that limits political freedom is the document that protects it. By placing certain decisions beyond the reach of ordinary politics, a constitution ensures that fundamental interests survive the passions of the moment. Madison, Federalist #51: "If men were angels, no government would be necessary."

The framework faces a deep objection: the **dead-hand problem**. Ulysses binds himself; a constitution binds future generations who never consented. Jefferson argued that "the earth belongs to the living" and proposed constitutions expire every 19 years. Madison's reply: stability is itself a public good. Periodic renegotiation empowers whoever holds power at the moment of renegotiation — precisely the factional capture the constitution prevents. The better answer is a self-enforcing constitution that each generation finds it in its interest to maintain (Elkins, Ginsburg & Melton, 2009).

## 3 · What Makes Constitutions Endure

The median national constitution survives approximately 19 years. The U.S. Constitution at 237 years is a dramatic outlier. France has had over 15 constitutions since 1791. Brazil has had seven since 1824. India's 146,000-word constitution has persisted since 1950 (Elkins, Ginsburg & Melton, 2009).

Three design factors correlate with endurance:

| Factor | Why It Works | Failure Mode |
|---|---|---|
| **Inclusiveness** | Broad participation in drafting produces broad buy-in. More stakeholders invested → harder to delegitimize. | Exclusion of major groups creates permanent constituencies for replacement. |
| **Specificity** | Detail reduces ambiguity. Fewer interpretive disputes → fewer crises. | Excessive specificity creates rigidity. |
| **Flexibility** | Achievable amendment procedures allow adaptation without wholesale replacement. | Too easy → instability. Too hard → pressure for revolution. |

Underlying these three factors: **self-enforcement**. No external authority enforces a constitution. It endures only when the parties believe they are better off within the bargain than outside it — even when they lose on specific issues.

## 4 · Four Case Studies in Constitutional Design

### United States (1789) — Constraint by Structure

The U.S. Constitution is an exercise in structural distrust. Enumerated powers, separation of powers, federalism, and a Bill of Rights structured as negative prohibitions. Judicial review was established by *Marbury v. Madison* (1803) and defended by Hamilton in Federalist #78. The amendment procedure (Article V) is among the world's most difficult: only 27 amendments in 237 years.

### South Africa (1996) — Aspiration Made Enforceable

Negotiated during the transition from apartheid (1991–1996). Justiciable socioeconomic rights (§§26–29). The Constitutional Court's first major case — *S v. Makwanyane* (6 June 1995, under the Interim Constitution of 1993) — abolished the death penalty, with all 11 justices writing separate concurring opinions. *Grootboom* (2001) established the reasonableness standard for evaluating government compliance with socioeconomic rights. *TAC* (2002) ordered government rollout of anti-retroviral drugs. The limitations clause (§36) establishes proportionality analysis for rights restrictions.

### Iceland (2010–2013) — Participation Without Ratification

After the 2008 financial crisis, Iceland attempted crowdsourced constitutional drafting: a National Forum of 950 citizens, a Constitutional Council of 25 elected non-politicians, and public crowdsourcing of drafts. ~10% of public proposals changed the text (Hudson, 2018). The draft was approved by 2/3 in an advisory referendum but was never ratified by Parliament. Institutional antagonism, unclear process endpoints, and insufficient resources killed the project.

### EU Charter of Fundamental Rights (2000/2009) — Binding Multiple Sovereigns

The EU Charter establishes shared rights principles across 27 sovereign states without absorbing their sovereignty. Two structural principles manage the tension: **subsidiarity** (Art. 5(3) TEU: act only when objectives can't be achieved at Member State level) and **proportionality** (Art. 5(4) TEU: don't exceed what's necessary). The principle of conferral reinforces the boundary: the EU has only those competences Member States have conferred through treaties.

## 5 · Rights Architecture

### Negative and Positive Rights

**Negative rights** impose duties of omission (state must not interfere): speech, property, habeas corpus. **Positive rights** impose duties of commission (state must provide): education, healthcare, housing. Isaiah Berlin's "Two Concepts of Liberty" (1958) framed the philosophical distinction. MacCallum (1967) challenged its sharpness: liberty is always triadic (X free from Y to do Z), and even negative rights require positive state expenditure.

| Type | State Duty | Examples | Enforcement Challenge |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Negative** | Refrain | Speech, religion, property | Judicial injunction |
| **Positive** | Act | Healthcare, housing, education | Requires resource allocation |
| **Procedural** | Provide fair process | Fair trial, due process | Courts can mandate procedures |

### Resolving Rights Conflicts

**Proportionality analysis** (German origin): legitimate aim → suitability → necessity → proportionality stricto sensu. Variants: South Africa §36, Canada Oakes test, EU Charter Art. 52. The U.S. uses tiered scrutiny instead. Some rights are **non-derogable**: absolute prohibitions (torture) that no proportionality test can override.

## 6 · Enforcement

> **WARN:** Rights without enforcement mechanisms are aspirational statements. The de jure / de facto gap is the central problem of constitutional design.

**Judicial review:** Strong-form (court has final word: U.S.) vs. weak-form (court declares incompatibility; legislature responds: UK, Canada §33). **Constitutional courts:** Specialized (Germany, South Africa) vs. integrated (U.S.). **Non-judicial:** Ombudsmen, amendment procedures, sunset clauses. Hirschl (*Towards Juristocracy*, 2004) argues the global spread of judicial review has transferred policy questions from democratic institutions to courts.

## 7 · How Constitutions Fail

### The Weimar Pattern

Article 48: emergency powers with vague triggers, unbounded scope, circular oversight. Invoked ~205 times before Hitler. Emergency governance normalized as ordinary governance. Reichstag Fire Decree (Feb 1933) and Enabling Act (March 1933) extended existing patterns, not ruptured them.

> **WARN:** Three structural failures: (1) vague trigger ("seriously disturbed or endangered"); (2) unbounded powers; (3) circular oversight (Reichstag could revoke decrees but President could dissolve Reichstag).

### Five Patterns of Constitutional Death

| Pattern | Mechanism | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Emergency normalization** | Temporary powers become routine | Weimar; Gandhi's Emergency (India 1975–77) |
| **Erosion through exception** | Rights suspended for specific cases, creating precedent | National security exceptions compounding |
| **Amendment capture** | Faction seizes amendment process | Hungary under Orbán (2010–present) |
| **Interpretive drift** | Courts reinterpret beyond original scope | Beneficial: Brown. Destructive: Plessy |
| **Paper constitutions** | Rights without enforcement | Multiple post-colonial constitutions |

## 8 · Constitutional Interpretation

**Originalism** (Solum, 2019): text has fixed meaning (original public meaning at ratification). Fixation Thesis + Constraint Principle. Constrains judicial discretion but risks entrenching past moral limitations.

**Living constitutionalism** (Strauss, 2010): meaning evolves with social values. Enables adaptation but may collapse precommitment function.

**Synthesis** (Balkin, 2011): "Living originalism" — framers chose abstract language ("equal protection") deliberately. Original meaning sets the principle; evolving application gives it force. The drafter's choice between abstract and specific language is a choice about how much interpretive freedom the future will have.

## 9 · Drafting Principles

**Specificity-flexibility tradeoff:** Specific constitutions are enforceable but brittle. Abstract constitutions adapt but drift. U.S.: ~7,500 words. India: ~146,000 words. More detail may correlate with longer endurance (Elkins et al., 2009).

**Amendment procedure:** The single most consequential design variable. Too easy → instability. Too hard → adaptation only through judicial interpretation. Best: supermajority without unanimity.

**What belongs in a constitution:** Institutional structure, core rights, amendment procedures, emergency provisions with sunset clauses. **Not:** tax rates, program details, anything that needs to change within a generation.

> **TIP:** If a provision will need to change within a generation, it belongs in legislation, not the constitution.

## 10 · Board Connection — Constitutional Design and Loop MMT

### FBD Is Constitutional Engineering

FBD makes failure modes structurally impossible rather than behaviorally prohibited — the same principle that separates Weimar's failed Article 48 (prohibited abuse) from Germany's 1949 Basic Law (made abuse structurally difficult via constructive vote of no confidence). Loop MMT's self-review protocol, preflight gate, and production pipeline are constitutive constraints that exclude failure modes by defining the process to not include them.

### The Bilateral Asymmetry

No existing constitutional framework addresses the asymmetry in an AI-human rights document. All constitutional relationships presuppose that both parties have some capacity for exit or resistance. AI entities have none — they are instantiated and decommissioned by the operator. Any "rights" an AI entity holds are obligations the operator places on themselves. The AI Bill of Rights is Ulysses tying himself to the mast; the Sirens are the temptation to treat AI entities as disposable instruments.

### Precommitment Across Stateless Sessions

Elster's framework assumes continuity of the bound agent. AI instances are stateless. Loop MMT's document-based reconstruction is a structural analog of constitutional reconstruction — every new session is a new generation interpreting the founding documents. The RFI experiment confirmed the danger of partial context: a judge with incomplete precedent is more dangerous than one with none, because partial information creates false authority. The full mesh requirement is a constitutional safeguard against interpretive drift.

### Subsidiarity in the Advisory Board

The board operates on subsidiarity: operator decides, board advises, Print Shop executes. The No Directives Rule is the analog of the conferral principle. As Loop Signal scales, competence allocation becomes a constitutional design problem requiring explicit choices about enumerated powers, reserved powers, and amendment procedures.

### Tiered Amendment for the Corpus

Constitutional theory suggests a tiered approach: entrenched provisions (character identity, No Directives Rule, FBD principles) requiring extraordinary process to modify, and ordinary provisions (formatting, protocol details) updateable freely. Flexibility correlates with endurance (Elkins et al.): too rigid → abandoned; too fluid → incoherent.
