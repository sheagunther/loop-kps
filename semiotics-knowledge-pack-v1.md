## About This Document

Consider two presentations of the same sentence. In the first, it appears as body text in the middle of a paragraph. In the second, it sits inside a bordered callout labeled "FBD Principle." The propositional content is identical. The cognitive effect is not. Why? Because the callout is a sign — and it operates through a system of meaning that has nothing to do with the words inside it. Semiotics provides the precise vocabulary for understanding this difference.

This pack covers the two foundational models of the sign (Saussure, Peirce), the orders of signification through which formatting carries authority (Barthes), the axes of selection and combination that make every design choice meaningful (paradigm and syntagm), the convention systems that make documents legible (codes, after Eco), the framing apparatus that shapes reading before it begins (paratext, after Genette), and the multimodal grammar of visual design (Kress & van Leeuwen). It is built for AI advisors working inside structured document systems. The goal: give advisors the language to explain how documents mean, not just what they say. Draws on 15 sources.

## Section 1 · The Sign — Two Traditions

### Saussure: The Dyadic Sign

Ferdinand de Saussure (1857–1913) defined the linguistic sign as the bond between a **signifier** — the sound-image, the mark on the page, the visual form — and a **signified** — the concept it evokes. The sign is neither the form alone nor the concept alone; it is the coupling. Both elements are psychological for Saussure: the signifier is not the physical sound but the mental impression of sound, and the signified is not a thing in the world but a concept in the mind. These ideas were published posthumously in *Course in General Linguistics* (1916), compiled by his students Bally and Sechehaye from lecture notes.

Three principles organize Saussure's theory. **Arbitrariness**: there is no natural or necessary link between signifier and signified — any phoneme sequence could denote any concept, and does across languages. **Differential value**: a sign means what it means not through some positive essence but through its differences from every other sign in the system. The word "dark" acquires its value by not being "dim," "gloomy," or "light." **Systematic structure**: signs exist within a system (*langue*) from which individual acts of expression (*parole*) draw. *Langue* is the abstract code shared by a community; *parole* is each specific utterance that invokes the code. A document is *parole* — a specific text — but it is interpretable only because writer and reader share the *langue* of the document's format.

### Peirce: The Triadic Sign

Charles Sanders Peirce (1839–1914), working from logic rather than linguistics, proposed a three-part model. A **sign** (representamen) represents an **object** to produce an **interpretant** — the effect or further sign that arises in the mind of the interpreter. The interpretant is not the interpreter; it is what the interpreter thinks or does in response to the sign. And because the interpretant is itself a sign, it can generate further interpretants in a recursive chain Peirce called **semiosis**.

Peirce distinguished the **immediate object** (the object as the sign presents it) from the **dynamic object** (the object as it actually exists, independent of any particular representation). A version number like "v13" presents the immediate object "thirteenth iteration"; the dynamic object is the entire revision history and current state of the document. The gap between immediate and dynamic object is where misunderstanding lives.

> **KEY: The foundational insight.** Both traditions converge on one point: meaning is not inherent in signs. It is produced by systems of relations, conventions, and interpretation. A document's formatting, labeling, and structure are not transparent containers for content — they are sign systems that must be learned, shared, and maintained. When they break down, the content inside them is misread.

## Section 2 · Icon, Index, Symbol

Peirce classified signs by how they relate to their objects — his most influential contribution outside specialist scholarship.

### Three Modes of Reference

An **icon** signifies by sharing a quality with its object — by resemblance. A portrait resembles the sitter; a map preserves the spatial structure of territory; a diagram preserves the relational structure of a system. Peirce subdivided icons into **images** (resemblance in quality), **diagrams** (resemblance in relations), and **metaphors** (resemblance in representational character). Diagrams matter most for document work: flowcharts, entity-relationship diagrams, and architectural schematics are all icons that signify through structural correspondence rather than visual likeness.

An **index** signifies through a real, existential connection — typically causal or physical. Smoke indexes fire. A weathervane indexes wind. A pointing finger indexes its target. Proper names index specific individuals. The defining property: an index directs attention. It says "look there" without necessarily describing what is there.

A **symbol** signifies through convention, habit, or social agreement. The word "cat" is connected to the animal only because English speakers agree on the pairing. Traffic lights, mathematical notation, legal terminology, and document formatting conventions are all symbolic: their meaning is learned, not inherent.

### Signs Are Usually Mixed

Pure instances of any type are rare. A photograph is iconic (resembles its subject) and indexical (caused by light from the subject). A word is symbolic (conventional) but its position in a sentence functions indexically (pointing to its grammatical role). Peirce recognized that even icons and indices are almost always partly symbolic — you must learn the conventions of map-reading before a map can function iconically.

> **NOTE: In structured documents.** A version number is an index — it has an existential connection to the document's revision chain. A callout border-color is a symbol — green means "tip" only by convention. A TOC sidebar is iconic (its hierarchy mirrors the document's structure) and indexical (scroll-tracking points to the current position). A skin-switcher is symbolic (the label "Winamp" has no inherent meaning) but the act of switching is indexical (it points to the existence of multiple presentation contexts).

## Section 3 · Orders of Signification

Roland Barthes (1915–1980), building on the linguist Louis Hjelmslev's framework of expression and content planes, proposed that meaning operates in layered orders. This is the conceptual engine for understanding why formatting carries meaning beyond propositional content.

### Denotation

The first order of signification is **denotation**: a signifier couples with a signified to produce a sign at the literal level. This is the dictionary definition, the "obvious" reading. The word "tree" denotes a woody plant. A bordered box in a document denotes a piece of text that has been set apart from the main flow. Denotation is the level at which there seems to be no interpretation — just recognition.

### Connotation

The second order is **connotation**: the entire first-order sign (signifier + signified) becomes a new signifier, which then attaches to a further signified — the cultural, emotional, and ideological associations. Connotation is where formatting starts doing its real work. The bordered box that *denotes* "set-apart text" *connotes* importance, exception, or authority, depending on its visual treatment and label. This connotative meaning is not in the content but in the reader's knowledge of the document's sign system.

Signs are more **polysemic** — more open to multiple readings — at the connotative level than at the denotative level. A reader who does not share the document's formatting code may correctly decode the denotation (there is a colored box with text) while entirely missing the connotation (this is a non-negotiable design constraint). Connotative failure is the most common failure mode in cross-organizational document sharing.

### Myth

Barthes used the term **myth** for connotation that operates ideologically — where historically contingent values are naturalized, made to seem inevitable and self-evident. In Barthes' technical sense, myth does not mean "false belief." It means a semiotic operation that transforms culture into nature, policy into physics, choice into constraint.

A document that consistently uses formal structure — numbered sections, typed callouts, version-controlled headers — mythologizes systematicity. The structure communicates: "this is not opinion; this is an engineered artifact." Whether or not the content justifies that level of authority, the formatting naturalizes it.

> **KEY: The motivating question answered.** Text in a paragraph and the same text in a callout labeled "FBD Principle" share their denotation — identical propositional content. They diverge at connotation: the callout connotes exception, urgency, non-negotiability. The label connotes membership in a numbered system of architectural constraints. At the mythological level, the callout naturalizes the idea that this statement is a property of the system's architecture rather than a preference of its designer. The formatting does not merely present the content. It *positions* it within a framework of authority — and it does so through semiotic mechanisms that operate whether or not anyone consciously notices.

### Barthes' Reversal

In *S/Z* (1970), Barthes reversed his earlier position: denotation, he argued, is not actually the primary, neutral level it appears to be. Rather, it is "the last of the connotations" — a reading so thoroughly naturalized that it no longer seems like an interpretation at all. Denotation pretends to be the bedrock of meaning, but it is itself the product of cultural convention.

This matters for document design. The "plain paragraph" format is not a neutral default. It is a format with its own connotations — continuity, ordinariness, non-exception — that has been so naturalized it is experienced as the absence of formatting. Every choice, including the choice not to use a callout, is a semiotic act.

## Section 4 · Paradigm & Syntagm

Saussure identified two dimensions along which meaning is organized. He called one "syntagmatic" and the other "associative"; the term **paradigmatic** was later popularized by Roman Jakobson (1896–1982) and is now standard. Together these two axes constitute the structural grid on which all meaning-making operates.

### Syntagm: The Axis of Combination

A **syntagm** is an ordered combination of signs whose meaning depends on both the elements present and their arrangement. A sentence is a syntagm of words. A document section is a syntagm of title, paragraphs, callouts, and references. Rearrange the elements and the meaning changes. Syntagmatic relations are **intratextual**: they connect signs that are co-present within the text. They encode "this-and-this-and-this."

### Paradigm: The Axis of Selection

A **paradigm** is a set of alternatives from which one sign is selected for a given position in a syntagm. In the sentence "the man cried," the final word was chosen from a paradigm including "sang," "died," and "laughed." Each alternative would alter the meaning. Paradigmatic relations are **intertextual**: they connect the sign that was chosen to the signs that were available but absent. They encode "this-or-this-or-this."

The paradigmatic choice signifies partly through contrast. What was *not* selected shapes the meaning of what *was*. A sign's value is determined by both its syntagmatic position (what it combines with) and its paradigmatic alternatives (what it was chosen instead of).

### The Insight for Document Design

Every formatting decision is a paradigmatic choice from a set of available presentation modes. Placing a statement in a `warn` callout rather than a `note`, a `key`, a `tip`, or a plain paragraph is a selection. The chosen mode means what it means partly through its own properties and partly through its contrast with the alternatives not used.

## Section 5 · Codes

A **code** is a system of conventions that couples signifiers with signifieds, enabling the production and interpretation of messages. Saussure's *langue* is a code; so are dress codes, genre conventions, and document formatting systems. Jakobson emphasized that communication depends on encoder and decoder sharing a code — otherwise the message is noise rather than signal.

### Denotational and Connotational Codes

Codes operate at both levels of signification. A **denotational code** maps signifiers to their literal meanings: a red border means "warning-type callout." A **connotational code** maps first-order signs to their cultural associations: the warning callout connotes non-negotiability, danger of non-compliance, structural constraint.

Jakobson identified the **metalingual function** of communication: language about the code itself. Style guides, format specifications, and glossaries are metalingual artifacts. They exist to teach the code.

### Eco: Overcoding, Undercoding, Aberrant Decoding

Umberto Eco (1932–2016) introduced three concepts for code boundaries. **Overcoding** is the layering of additional, more specific rules on top of an existing code. **Undercoding** is provisional interpretation without established rules. **Aberrant decoding** results when encoder and decoder apply different codes to the same message.

> **WARN: Aberrant decoding in document systems.** Aberrant decoding is the primary risk when structured documents cross organizational boundaries. A reader from a different formatting tradition may interpret a "note" callout as a "warning," read a version number as a chapter number, or treat a skin-switcher as a cosmetic toy rather than a signal of presentation-independence. The countermeasure is metalingual documentation: make the code explicit.

## Section 6 · Paratext

Gérard Genette (1930–2018) introduced the concept of **paratext** to describe the apparatus surrounding a text that mediates it to the reader. Genette, quoting Philippe Lejeune, described paratext as a "fringe of the printed text which in reality controls one's whole reading of the text."

### Peritext and Epitext

**Peritext** is the paratextual material within the same artifact: titles, chapter headings, tables of contents, notes, typography, formatting decisions, version numbers, build tags, copyright notices.

**Epitext** is the paratextual material outside the artifact: reviews, handoff documents, status reports, correspondence, any external discourse about the text.

Paratext = peritext + epitext.

> **NOTE: Six functions of peritext.** (1) Production — who made it, when. (2) Promotion — how it is positioned. (3) Navigation — how to move through it. (4) Intratextual — internal cross-references. (5) Supplementary — additional material. (6) Documentation — sources and provenance.

### Paratext Is Not Neutral

Paratext does not merely frame content; it actively conditions how the content is received. Genette described paratext as a "zone of transaction" between text and reader. It instructs the reader on *how* to read the text — and this instruction is effective precisely because it is not experienced as instruction. It is experienced as context.

## Section 7 · Multimodal Semiotics

Gunther Kress (1940–2019) and Theo van Leeuwen (1947–) argued that actual communication deploys multiple **modes** simultaneously: language, image, layout, typography, color, and spatial arrangement. A document page is not a linguistic text decorated with visuals; it is a multimodal artifact in which every element carries meaning.

### Visual Grammar

Drawing on Halliday's systemic functional linguistics, Kress and van Leeuwen proposed three metafunctions of visual communication:

**Representational meaning** — what the visual is about.

**Interactive meaning** — the relationship the visual establishes between producer and viewer. Monospaced fonts position the reader as technical. Generous whitespace positions them as someone whose time is being respected.

**Compositional meaning** — how elements are organized into a coherent whole. In Western left-to-right cultures, left = given, right = new; top = ideal, bottom = real.

### Design as Semiotic Act

Every layout decision, font choice, and color assignment is a meaning-making act — whether or not the designer is conscious of the meanings produced.

## Section 8 · Semiosis & Its Limits

**Semiosis** is the process by which signs produce meaning. In Peirce's model it is recursive: each interpretant is itself a sign generating further interpretants, in principle without end. Eco called this **unlimited semiosis**.

In practice, communities arrest semiosis through codes, conventions, and what Peirce called **habit**. A well-designed document format constrains semiosis enough for the intended meaning to be reliably recovered, without constraining it so tightly that the document cannot be read in new contexts.

Charles Morris (1901–1979) divided the study of signs into three useful branches: **syntactics** (formal relations among signs), **semantics** (relations between signs and what they represent), and **pragmatics** (relations between signs and their interpreters).

## Section 9 · Board Connection — Document Semiotics in Practice

**Callout Types as a Paradigm.** The L21 callout system defines a paradigm of four: `tip`, `warn`, `key`, `note`. Each type signifies through its own properties and through contrast with the alternatives. Adding a fifth type shifts the values of all four, because value is differential.

**Document Format as Code.** L21 is a semiotic code coupling visual signifiers with signifieds. The format specification is a metalingual document teaching the code by which other documents should be read.

**Version Numbers as Indices.** "v13" and "9 April 2026" have existential connections to the document's history. They are production peritext in Genette's terms.

**The Skin System as Myth.** OG/Winamp/Sunrise denotes visual preference. It connotes adaptability. At the mythological level, it naturalizes the separation of content and presentation.

**FBD Principles: Three Orders at Once.** First order: highlighted text. Second order: membership in a numbered constraint system. Third order: naturalization of design decisions as architectural properties.

**Event Ledgers as Syntagms.** Append-only order is a syntactic rule defining legal operations within a sequential chain.

**Handoffs as Epitext.** EOD handoffs exist outside the documents but mediate them to the next reader. Bad epitext produces aberrant decoding.

> **TIP: For advisors.** When an operator asks "why does this formatting matter?" or "can't we just use plain text?", you now have precise answers. Formatting is not decoration. It is a sign system carrying connotative and mythological meaning, positioning the reader relative to the content's authority, constraining polysemy through a shared code, and providing paratextual framing that controls the reading act before it begins. Changing the formatting does not change how content looks. It changes what the content *means*.
